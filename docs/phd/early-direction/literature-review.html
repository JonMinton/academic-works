Conceptually develops from Bourdieu’s anthropological research on hunter-gatherer societies within Algeria. Sociology, and appreciated by postmodern academics due to its French authorship and use of a Marxian vocabulary. But at the same uses empirical evidence and methodologies and is referenced favourably by the evolutionary psychologist Steven Pinker in the Blank Slate. Therefore could be useful as ‘bridging’ material. The extension of anthropological ideas to modern societies suggests Bourdieu implicitly believes in a common human behavioural ‘core’, rather than in us as beings that are infinitely socially malleable. This suggests he’s more of a ‘tragic realist’/pluralist, rather than a cultural relativist. Explores social value of items as indicators of class membership. Attempts to provide some kind of empirical methodology with which to test Kant’s Critique of Judgement. Uses survey data to show a pattern between social activity – as evidenced by indicators such as museum attendance - and education (formal qualifications); and between education and social origin (e.g. father’s occupation). The former suggests an implicit practical logic to social activities as a way of signalling class membership. The latter suggests the extent to which classes reproduce themselves. The surveys were conducted in the ‘60s in France, so the data themselves are unlikely to be useful. Just the underlying principles and concepts. The Law of Large Numbers “…for a heady half century or so there seemed to be no demarcations between physics, mathematics, economics, politics and sociology.” The Law of Large Numbers and the discovery (by Gauss and LaPlace) of distributions underlying large numbers of independent events which, given enough observations, reveal regularities.*

The Grand Ah-Whoom Considers abrupt changes in phenomena, which are inherent to the nature of the phenomena, but which are not always deducible from the behaviour of the material within which the phenomena exists, but which are not always inductively predictable by observation of the phenomena.Such phenomena undergophase transitions, small changes in an input variable, which under most conditions lead to a proportionate change in the output variable, lead instead to a disproportionate change in the output variable when they occur at the edge of a phase. The archetypal example of material whose phenomena undergo phase transitions is water, whose properties change dramatically around 0 and 100 degrees centigrade (by definition).

Uncommon Proportions Suggests many socioeconomic phenomena follow a ‘power-law’ pattern, which emerges from local interaction effects and leads to is modelled within catastrophe theory. The Work of Many Hands Discusses microsimulation modelling by political scientists such as Robert Axtell which seek to reproduce the macroscale pattern of business numbers and sizes (the power law described earlier). The inference is that, if the microsimulation can reproduce observed patterns by positing certain axioms about individual-level behaviour, the individual-level behaviour being posited possibly bears some (much simplified) resemblance to reality.

Join the Club ‘Setting Standards’: Provides one of many descriptions of “The QWERTY Effect” – the establishment of suboptimal norms through satisficing and path-dependency. Quite often, many possible solutions to problem reduce to just one or two solutions, because having many solutions to a problem is itself a problem: it leads to problems with inefficiency caused by an inability to benefit from economies of scale due to incompatibilities between solution-systems. This leads to a strong desire to arrive quickly at a satisfactory standard. Once a standard has begun to gain some popularity, its increased popularity is itself considered a reason to increase its popularity. This is an example ofautocatalys, orpositive feedback. Eventually the solution-space narrows onto just one or two solutions, other configurations of the ‘landscape’ are not stable for long periods of time. Once a stable configuration of the solution landscape has been established, the landscape is often very resilient to change. The established solutions – VHS rather than Betamax, QWERTY rather than something more efficient – become historical legacies which often become increasingly suboptimal and inefficient relative to possible new solutions which arise. Eventually, the difference in efficiency between the historical solution and the new solution becomes so great that cultural conservatism/lock-in is overpowered and the new solution usurps the dominant position occupied by the previous solution.

‘Head for the Valleys’: This describes how this approach has been formalised and used by the political scientist Robert Axelrod, under the termLandscape Theory, which is derived from statistical physics. Multitudes in the Valley of Decision Physical science’s most important message to social science: “do not be tempted too readily into extrapolating from the psychology of the individual to the behaviour of the group”

‘Cast your Vote’ Describes voting patterns as being based on local interaction (‘herd mentality’ effects) and following a power-law (linear on a log-log graph)

‘Which Way to Turn?’ Joshua Epstein suggests many conventions and norms are established within society so as to reduce the number of decisions people have to make: neither driving on the left nor the right side of the road is intrinsically preferable, what is preferable is that everyone drives on thesameside of the road, irrespective of which side that happens to be. “Epstein suggests that we structure society so as to keep the need for thought to a minimum.” (p. 384)

‘Worlds Apart’ Describes Schelling’s approach in his 1978 bookMicromotives and Macrobehaviourin some detail. Develops the racial segregation model referencing a computer model created by Paul Ormerod: ‘A preference to mix with people similar to oneself is as old as humanity’.

‘Crime and Punishment’ This section describes Campbell and Ormerod’s ‘tipping point’ model to crime – which presents the effects of two variables (punitive and socioeconomic) as having a nonlinear relationship to crime levels. It relates it, of course, to Malcolm Gladwell’s speculation, inThe Tipping Point, as to the reasons for declines in crime levels in New York City.

‘Which Way to Turn?’
Joshua Epstein suggests many conventions and norms are established within society so as to reduce the number of decisions people have to make: neither driving on the left nor the right side of the road is intrinsically preferable, what is preferable is that everyone drives on the*same*side of the road, irrespective of which side that happens to be. “Epstein suggests that we structure society so as to keep the need for thought to a minimum.” (p. 384)

‘Worlds Apart’
Describes Schelling’s approach in his 1978 book*Micromotives and Macrobehaviour*in some detail. Develops the racial segregation model referencing a computer model created by Paul Ormerod: ‘A preference to mix with people similar to oneself is as old as humanity’.

*‘Crime and Punishment’*
This section describes Campbell and Ormerod’s ‘tipping point’ model to crime – which presents the effects of two variables (punitive and socioeconomic) as having a nonlinear relationship to crime levels. It relates it, of course, to Malcolm Gladwell’s speculation, in*The Tipping Point*, as to the reasons for declines in crime levels in New York City.

This, of course, is the type of pattern Kuhn described to 'scientific revolutions': a new approach to a problem has much more instrumental capacity - capacity to change the environment and control a natural phenomena (or subset of a natural phenomena) in a way which someone wants. This increased instrumentalist capacity is recognised and investors begin to pour more resources into the new solution (or 'paradigm') until the only the new solution is funded and the original solution 'dies out'. Though persons aren't 'converted' from one ideology to another, something - which*could*be called progress - happens within science and technology.

This is something which could be considered within the structures of Game Theory. This approach is, of course, crypto-Darwinian, so has a rather hard-edged feel to it.
