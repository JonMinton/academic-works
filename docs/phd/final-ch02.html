Chapter 2: Organisms, Decisions and Emotions: Some Theories of Very Small Scale Social Phenomena

Source: Pages 23–39 of MintonThesis.pdf (September 2009). Text extracted from PDF; figures extracted directly as images.

2.1 Introduction

The topics covered within this chapter are perhaps the least intuitive of those covered within the entire thesis, and as such this chapter may be more challenging than many of those that follow. Paradoxically, this is because the contents of this chapter include the topic of intuition itself: what it is and how it emerged. This is because having intuitions – as well as feelings, suspicions, beliefs, thoughts, and habits – is part of what it means to be members of a particular type of complex organism (human beings), and this chapter is about organisms, both in the more specific sense of the human organism, and in a more general sense. It considers the ontology, the essential features and qualities, of ‘the organism’ as a particular way of thinking about structures and relationships between elements. The purposes of this chapter include:

  1. To highlight the extent to which conscious acts such as thinking, seeing, imagining, and deciding are related to, and dependent upon, unconscious acts such as breathing, sensing, feeling, and responding.
  2. To suggest the mutually interdependent relationship between ‘higher’ mental functions (those of which we are conscious) and ‘lower’ mental functions (those of which we are not conscious), and thus to make the case that, in order to understand the operation of ‘higher’ functions, ‘lower’ functions must also be understood.
  3. Following from the previous point, to illustrate the importance of emotional responses in decision-making; and conversely, to present the theory that aspects of the brain and body function as emotional ‘simulators’ (or emotional-stimulus replicators).
  4. To suggest some adaptive benefits, and thus the selective forces, that may lead to the development of ‘higher’ mental functions.
  5. To illustrate the extent to which the majority of individuals’ behaviours, actions, and performances are automated and unconscious, rather than consciously evaluated and pre-selected; as a corollary, to indicate the extent to which consciousness – and choice-making (a conscious act) – may be thought of, from the perspective of the organism, as a ‘burden’ to be avoided where possible, rather than as something to be ‘sought’ or ‘maximised’.
  6. To present a conceptual distinction, and description of the relationship, between the ‘organism’ and the ‘environment’; and between the ‘subject’ and the ‘object’.

In order to avoid having to do the additional work of developing a conceptual and terminological lexicon, as well as learning a new set of theories, this discussion will be anchored around the theories and descriptions provided by a single theorist, the neuroscientist Antonio Damasio, and on accounts presented within his book, The Feeling of What Happens.1

By detailing the essential qualities of organisms, I will present not just a relatively novel description of social beings, but also a set of concepts and associated terminology, derived from analysis of one kind of emergent phenomena, which may be of use in later chapters when attempting to understand other phenomena. In particular, within later chapters I will attempt to identify similarities between the functioning of (very-small scale) biological organisms and (large scale) bureaucratic organisations. Illustrative questions include:

To what extent do legal documents perform similar tasks within organisations as emotions do within organisms?

To what extent do organisational roles and management hierarchies have similar information-processing qualities as those observed within organisms?

To what extent do both organisms and organisations ‘learn’, and become ‘habituated’ to respond to particular forms of (external) environmental stimuli in the same ways?

To what extent are organisations like organisms in their quest for self-replication and self-preservation?

2.2 Health Inequalities and Human Physiology

To begin with, consider the following description of the ‘psychosocial model’ of health inequalities:

The original ideas behind [the psychosocial model] were based on the existence of two types of response to external threats shared by both humans and other animals. … The most well known … is the so called ‘fight or flight’ response. The body received alarm messages from the brain that can be thought of as activating one or other of two ‘response circuits’ […]

[The sympathetic-adrenomedullary circuit] involves the sympathetic nervous system, which, in balance with the parasympathetic nervous system, is responsible for much of what goes on in a routine way in the body without conscious awareness. They govern how fast the heart is beating, response to changes in outside temperature (shivering, sweating) and the pH (acidity) level of the blood, for example. Sympathetic nervous stimulation releases adrenaline from the adrenal medulla, and at the same time the ends of the sympathetic nerves themselves release noradrenaline. This prompts the release into the bloodstream of fibrinogen, a substance that helps blood to clot more easily. At the same time, the heart rate and blood pressure rise, and the small blood vessels (capillaries) nearest to the skin narrow sharply. This would prevent excessive blood loss from a wound, but it also has the effect of raising the blood pressure.2

To use terms and concepts which will be described in more detail later in the chapter, one could say the following: the release of fibrinogen, the increase in heart rate and blood pressure, the narrowing of capillaries – are all automatic, unconscious components of what Damasio calls “complex, stereotyped patterns of response”,3 known collectively as an ‘emotion’ – and are the chronic, proximate causes of heart diseases which exhibit a well observed and consistent social gradient. One can treat such proximate causes – for example, through the consumption of aspirin, which thins the blood – in order to counter the ‘abnormal’, physiological imbalance of the human organism. However, following the logic of the psychosocial hypothesis, this ‘abnormality’, within the blood of the organism, results directly from a normal response to an abnormal environment.

From the perspective to be developed within this chapter, the above can be understood by thinking about the relationship between a biological organism and an aspect of the environment inhabited by the organism; for reasons to be made clear, we can refer to ‘the aspect of the environment’ as an ‘object’, and name this particular type of object a ‘Stressor’.

The biological organism is in this case a ‘human being’, one of ‘us’ at the most fundamental physiological, emotional, cognitive and existential level. We would thus lose something of the substantive implications of this perspective if we were to refer to this organism in a way which implies an ontological ‘otherness’ (‘they’, ‘it’). Instead, as ‘we’ are ‘the organism’, the above passage, a technical description of human physiology, begins to assume a tragic quality: We are made conscious of our emotional responses to different social situations, in order that we can make decisions about how to act in order to move away from the negative environmental aspects – ‘Stressors’, in this case – and towards positive environmental aspects. However, we exist in a social environment where such Stressors are all-pervasive. Our awareness of our emotions means that we know we should move away from the Stressors, but the environment is such that we cannot.

The use of the first-person plural within the above discussion is intended to suggest both what may be at stake, and what might be gained, from temporarily adopting an organism-centred perspective to social and societal issues, as opposed to the more intuitive individual- and group-centred perspectives we are likely to adopt in every-day life. Once the behaviours and sensations of the individual, and the sense of being an individual, are understood to be largely explicable in terms of, and often appear consequences of, the properties and functions of the organism, then the extent to which being able to make a gestalt shift in perspective, from that of the individual, to that of the organism, can aid our understanding of social, sociological, and societal phenomena becomes clearer.

2.3 The Individual and the Organism

To help make this transition, I will briefly consider an apparently esoteric and abstract problem usually addressed only by philosophers: the nature (and the notion) of ‘self’. In Bertrand Russell’s History of Western Philosophy, when discussing the French philosopher Rene Descartes’ famous assertion “Cogito, ergo sum” (“I think, therefore I am”), he suggests the statement is problematic because ‘I’ (the ‘-o’ part of ‘cogito’) is undefined. Instead, suggests Russell, the statement should simply assert that “there are thoughts”.4 William James, a few decades earlier, made a similar observation within his book The Principles of Psychology.5

It is astonishing what havoc is wrought in psychology by admitting at the outset apparently innocent suppositions that nevertheless contain a flaw. The bad consequences develop themselves later on, and are irredeemable, being woven through the whole texture of the work. … The only thing which psychology has a right to postulate at the outset is the fact of thinking itself, and that must first be taken up and analyzed. […]

The first fact for us, then, as psychologists, is that thinking of some sort goes on. … If we could say in English ‘it thinks,’ as we say ‘it rains’ or ‘it blows,’ we should be stating the fact most simply and with the minimum of assumption. As we cannot, we must simply that that thought goes on.

If accepting the concept of self is more problematic than accepting the concept of thinking, then this raises a new question: What is it that is doing the thinking? The problem of what is thinking led Damasio to suggest the following:

How do we ever know that we are seeing a given object? How do we become conscious in the full sense of the word? How is the sense of self in the act of knowing implanted in the mind? The way into a possible answer for the questions on self came only after I began seeing the problem of consciousness in terms of two key players, the organism and the object, and in terms of the relationships those players hold in the course of their natural interactions. The organism in question is that within which consciousness occurs; the object in question is any object that gets to be known in the consciousness process; and the relationships between organism and object are the contents of the knowledge we call consciousness. Seen in this perspective, consciousness consists of constructing knowledge about two facts: that the organism is involved in relation to some object, and that the object in the relation causes a change in the organism.6

When a change occurs within an organism, as a result of its relationship with a particular object, Damasio suggests the organism experiences an ‘image’ of the object. In this case, he uses the term ‘image’ as a synonym for ‘mental pattern’,7 and by this usage an ‘image’ generalises beyond visual patterns, referring equally, for example, to tactile patterns (the sensation of being touched by a feather, or of a spider crawling along one’s skin), auditory patterns (the sound of a police siren speeding away on a nearby street), olfactory patterns (the smell of roses or fetid gorgonzola), or any other form of sensory pattern.

The organism does not have to be conscious of an image, or even be capable of consciousness, in order to experience the image, because an image of an object is simply the organism’s subjective response to the object. (Therefore, if there were no organisms, there would be no objects, and no images.) There are some images that the organism is conscious of experiencing, and others that the organism is not. Whether subjectively experienced or not, biological correlates of images may – in theory, but not always in practice – be observed by third parties. Examples of such correlates include: changes in the skin’s electrical conductance; changes in the form – pattern, shape, intensity, duration – of electromagnetic field surrounding the skull. From Damasio’s perspective, all subjective mental images could, in theory, be shown to be perfectly correlated with a given objective pattern of biological activity.

2.4 Organisms and Boundaries

The term ‘organism’ itself is perhaps even more difficult, but at least as important, to understand. Most fundamentally, the concept of an ‘organism’ (much like that of an ‘organisation’, covered later in the thesis), is dependent upon “the definition of their boundary, the separation between that is in and what is out.”8

The structure of the organism is inside the boundary and the life of the organism is defined by the maintenance of internal states within the boundary. Singular individuality depends on the boundary.9

Damasio then states that “even when large variations occur in the environment that surrounds an organism, there is a dispositional arrangement in the organism’s structure that modifies the inner workings of the organism.”10

The dispositional arrangement ensures that the environmental variations do not cause a correspondingly large and excessive variation of activity within. When variations that trespass into a dangerous range are about to occur, they can be averted by some preemptive action; and where dangerous variations have already occurred, they can still be corrected by some appropriate action.11

The process by which a living system or organism comes to maintain its non-equilibrium stable state is called homeostasis. As Damasio explains:

Homeostasis refers to the coordinated and largely automated physiological reactions required to maintain stable internal states in a living organism. Homeostasis describes the automatic regulation of temperature, oxygen concentration, or pH in your body.12

From this perspective, organisms are thus fundamentally a form of homeostatic system, which use a series of internal feedback loops to self-regulate and maintain a non-equilibrium stable state with respect to a variety of qualities that exist within the organism. Once the internal structure of the organism loses its functional integrity, and can no longer maintain the differential boundary between itself and that which surrounds it, then, by this definition, it ceases to be alive.

2.5 Organisms as Abstract Machines

In asserting that the organism functions as a homeostatic system, the claim is implicitly being made that the organism represents a particular form of – to use a term Manuel De Landa attributes to Giles Deleuze and Felix Guattari – ‘abstract machine.’13 This may be thought of, heuristically, as a formal specification of a set of relationships between components, but not of the materials out of which the components are constructed. For example, both an unopened bottle of wine and an unopened tin can may be thought of as different instantiations of the same abstract machine ‘closed container’, which defines a relationship – that may be formally specified topologically – between three components: ‘contents’, ‘boundary’, and ‘outside’. These abstract machine components may be thought to ‘exist’ to the extent that one can perform isomorphic ‘mapping’ between substrates: ‘the glass is to the bottle as the tin is to the can’, ‘the wine is to the bottle as the baked beans are to the can’, and so on. The names given to enterprises which attempt to describe and understand this form of abstract machine, the homeostatic system, include ‘systems theory’, ‘control theory’ and ‘cybernetics’.

To recapitulate: from a cybernetics perspective, a system could be considered to be ‘alive’ if it manages, through feedback loops and mechanisms within its internal structure, to maintain a non-equilibrium stable state, with regard to a set of definable and measurable properties, and with respect to that which is external to it (the environment). The ‘equilibrium’ refers to the normal state of the environment with respect to those properties by which the ‘living system’ and environment differ. Functionally, ‘the boundary’, by which the living system or organism is defined, refers to a significant and systematic difference between the conditions of a given range of properties – moisture content, pH levels, electrical activity and so on – between that which is outside (the environment) and that which is inside (the organism).

2.6 Objects and Emotions

An object is an aspect of the environment that has been differentiated from others by an organism. Even without consciousness, the organism can objectify aspects of the environment by producing systematically different responses to different environmental aspects (i.e. produce images of the objects). For example, towards some aspects of the environment, the organism may shrink, and to others it may swell. In this example the former environmental aspect would be, from the perspective of the organism, a ‘shrink-inducing’ object, whereas the latter would be a ‘swell-inducing’ object.

At the most basic level, an emotion is a response to an object. Within the hypothesised organism above, shrinking and swelling are, effectively, emotions. Damasio asserts that “emotions are part and parcel of the regulations we call homeostasis.”14 In the Shrink-Sweller, one may presume, the emotional responses of shrinking and swelling may allow better regulation of core temperature when the external temperature of the environment decrease, in the cases of shrinking, or increases rapidly, in the case of swelling.

2.7 Feelings and Self

Decision-making requires a conscious, reasoning self. Damasio suggests the faculties for reason and consciousness emerge atop a biological hierarchy of increasingly complex biological and regulatory processes, as shown in Figure 1 below.

Note that the arrows of causal influence, from highest to lowest level, run in both directions. In order for an emotion to become a feeling, there needs to exist the biological machinery for signalling, in one part of the organism, that an emotion is occurring in another part. Such signals can then be relayed to the non-emotive part of the organism, and the appropriate emotional response generated.

To continue with the example of the Shrink-Sweller, a shrink-inducing object may induce an emotional response through direct contact with one side of the organism. This change in the internal state of that side of the organism could then, through a sequence of internal causal influence, lead to a similar emotional response within the rest of the organism. If the sequence of internal causal influence is systematic in its behaviour in response to the change in the emotional state within an aspect of the organism, then this causal sequence functions as a signalling mechanism within the organism. This internal signalling, in response to emotional change, is the core quality of feeling. This particular pattern of signalling responses, resulting from the organism’s contact with the object, is what Damasio refers to as an image; the Shrink-Sweller organism thus has an ‘image’ of shrink-inducing objects, and an ‘image’ of swell-inducing objects.

2.8 Centralisation and the Proto-self

An early prerequisite for the sense of self is the emergence of a complex, holistic system of emotional identification and signalling within the organism. This is the function of the central nervous system. With internal sensory centralisation, the emotional responses – complex, unconscious behaviours – of the entire organism can come to be coordinated, and where such coordination of activity is evolutionary advantageous such trends towards sensory centralisation develop. The sense of self depends upon the capacity of the organism to behave coherently as a single entity, and respond systematically, effectively and in a coordinated fashion to changes in any of its aspects.

When an organism becomes centrally connected in this way, it becomes meaningful to consider the organism to be an object with respect to itself. (To recall, an ‘object’ has here been defined as something which may have systematic influence upon the organism. With a central nervous system, which relays changes in the emotional state of any aspect of the organism, the organism comes to yield a coherent, systematic, unified response upon itself.)

2.9 Emotions and Decision-making

Given the above discussion, I am now in a better position to start to understand the process of decision-making, and the relationship between decision-making and emotions. This relationship should not be underestimated. As Damasio states, “emotion is integral to the processes of reasoning and decision making, for worse and for better.”15

From this perspective, the faculty of decision-making relates intimately to the prediction, generation, and evaluation of differing emotional states under a variety of hypothesised environmental circumstances. The capacity to reason is thus ultimately dependent upon, and in service to, the capacity to know and compare feelings. The faculty for reason thus does not, and cannot, exist without emotion (which itself cannot exist without complex life). As Damasio states, “the engines of reason still require emotion, which means that the controlling power of reason is often modest.”16

Thoughts, elements of the reasoning process, come to generate dispositional memories of how the organism, the biological predicate of the self, tends to respond to a given object.

There is no such thing as pure perception of an object within a sensory channel … They perceive an object … the organism requires both specialized sensory signals and signals from the adjustment of the body, which are necessary for perception to occur. […]

The statement also holds true when you are simply thinking of an object rather than actually perceiving it in the world outside your organism. … The records we hold of the objects and events that we once perceived include the motor adjustments we made to obtain the perception in the first place and also include the emotional reactions we had then. [Emphasis added]. [E]ven when we “merely” think about an object, we tend to reconstruct memories not just of a shape or color but also of the perceptual engagement the objects required and of the accompanying emotional reactions, regardless of how slight. … [T]he images you form in your mind always signal to the organism its own engagement with the business of making images and evoke some emotional reactions. You simply cannot escape the affectation of your organism, motor and emotional most of all, that is part and parcel of having a mind.17

[…]

Now let us turn to the case of an object that is not actually present but has, rather, been committed to memory. According to my framework, the memory of that object has been stored in dispositional form. Dispositions are records which are dormant and implicit rather than active and explicit, as images are. Those dispositional memories of an object that was once actually perceived include not only records of the sensory aspects of the object … but also records of the motor adjustments that necessarily accompanies the gathering of the sensory signals; moreover the memories also contain records of the obligate emotional reaction to the object. As a consequence, when we recall an object, when we allow dispositions to make their implicit information explicit, we retrieve not just sensory data but also accompanying motor and emotional data. When we recall an object, we recall not just sensory characteristics of an actual object but the past reactions of the organism to that object.18

The image of the object is re-imagined, and through this the dispositional tendencies of the organism in response to the object come to be remembered (and thus to some extent involuntarily ‘performed’).

2.10 The As-if-body-loop

If mindfulness and consciousness perform, ultimately, the function of selecting from a limited range of potential behaviours, a limited proportion of the time, it follows that the vast majority of one’s behaviours are not consciously determined at all. Instead, they exist as unconscious, predetermined patterns of behaviour, of varying levels of complexity, in response to particular ‘stimuli’. If this process of remembering were to continually involve the entire body, the process could be expensive, in terms of both time and other resources (because, in this case, deciding would require rapid and substantial alteration of the body’s electro-chemical and physiological functioning). Thus, in order to make the process of reasoning ‘cheaper’, faculties of the brain develop which simulate the responses of the rest of the body to a given object.

Thoughts entering this ‘as-if-body-loop’, as Damasio calls it, thus induce the same kinds of response within the periphery of the brain, from the perspective of the decision-making aspects of the brain, as would comparable signals from the brain to the rest of the body. Much of the brain thus behaves as a kind of emotional simulator, a cognitive laboratory under which various what-if scenarios, involving different relationships between objects, may be compared in order to assess their likely effects upon the organism.

2.11 From Memories and Dispositions to Habits and Habitus

The previous sections have described, from a neurological perspective, how decisions are made. In doing so, it has illustrated the extent to which unconscious processes pre-dominate both our responses to, and understanding of, our environment, and our conscious decisions to aspects thereof. Understanding individual and group behaviours may often be more a matter of understanding the decisions people don’t make than the decisions that they do.

Recall from Figure 1 that the majority of processes performed by the human organism are below the level of consciousness, and also that the causal arrows – from ‘high’ to ‘low’ processes – go in both directions. This makes sense if one thinks about it: bring to one’s mind and linger upon a vivid memory of a time one was scared, and within seconds this act of ‘high reason’ has permeated downwards through the levels, re-kindling a sense of the feeling of fear (initially just within the as-if-body loop), then inducing a genuine whole-body emotion, which in turn leads to changes in the performance of aspects of one’s basic physiology (faster breathing, greater perspiration, and so on); the low level physiological change may then permeate back upwards, heightening the strength of the emotional response, which then amplifies the feeling (which now occurs throughout the entire body, rather than just within the as-if-body loop), which in turn makes the fearful memory even more vivid.

Conscious acts also enter the realm of the unconscious by generating dispositional tendencies within the organism; more colloquially, these may be termed ‘habits’, and may be thought of as complex patterns of behaviour acquired through experiences. Although the division is far from absolute, the capacity to acquire habits or dispositions is something that separates people from most other types of organism. As William James wrote:

Man19 is born with a tendency to do more things than his has ready-made arrangements for in his nerve-centres. Most of the performances of other animals are automatic. But in him the number of them is so enormous, that most of them must be the fruit of painful study. If practice did not make perfect, nor habit economize the expense of nervous and muscular energy, he would therefore be in a sorry plight.20

Being ‘too conscious’, therefore, of the vast and complex array of repertoires one needs to perform in order to function on a day-to-day basis, is likely to severely impede the extent to which one can perform such repertoires, and so function effectively. And as James then explained: “habit diminishes the conscious attention with which our acts are performed.”21 He continues:

One may state this abstractly thus: If an act requires for its execution a chain, A, B, C, D, E, F, G, etc., of successive nervous events, then in the first performances of the action the conscious will must choose each of these events from a number of wrong alternatives that tend to present themselves; but habit soon brings it about that each event calls up its own appropriate successor without any alternative offering itself, and without any references to the conscious will, until at last the whole chain, A, B, C, D, E, F, G, rattles itself off as soon as A occurs, just as if A and the rest of the chain were fused into a continuous stream.22

As a tangible example of the above process, James offered the following illustration:

The writer well remembers how, on revisiting Paris after ten years’ absence, and, findings himself in the street in which for one winter he had attended school, he lost himself in a brown study, from which he was awakened by finding himself upon the stairs which led to the apartment in a house many streets away in which he had lived during that earlier time, and to which his steps from the school had then habitually led. We all of us have a definite routine manner of performing certain daily offices […]. Our lower centres know the order of these movements, and show their knowledge by their ‘surprise’ if the objects are altered so as to oblige the movement to be made in a different way. But our higher thought-centres know hardly anything about the matter.23

Just as James’ discussion of habits can be called upon to highlight the relationship between ‘higher functions’ of the human organism and every-day individual behaviours and experiences, so Pierre Bourdieu’s concept of habitus can be used to link individual habits with social groups. As an editor’s introduction to a volume of Bourdieu’s essays describes the concept:

The habitus is a set of dispositions which incline agents to act and react in certain ways. The dispositions generate practices, perceptions and attitudes which are ‘regular’ without being consciously co-ordinated or governed by any ‘rule’. The dispositions which constitute the habitus are inculcated, structured, durable, generative and transposable – features that each deserve a brief explanation. Dispositions are acquired through a gradual process of inculcation in which early childhood experiences are particularly important. Through a myriad of mundane processes of training and learning … the individual acquires a set of dispositions which literally mould the body and become second nature. The dispositions produced thereby are also structured in the sense that they unavoidably reflect the social conditions within which they are acquired. … [T]he similarities and differences that characterize the social conditions of existence of individuals will be reflected in the habitus, which may be relatively homogeneous across individuals from similar backgrounds. Structured dispositions are […] ingrained in the body in such a way that they endure through the life history of the individual, operating in a way that is pre-conscious and hence not readily amenable to conscious reflection and modification [and] are generative and transposable in the sense that they are capable of generating a multiplicity of practices and perceptions in fields other than those in which they were originally acquired. As a durably installed set of dispositions, the habitus tends to generate practices and perceptions, works and appreciations, which concur with the conditions of existence of which the habitus is itself the product.24

In any case, the extent to which broader social factors systematically shape individual habits and dispositions, and the extent to which the capacity for persons to embody dispositions is dependent upon the qualities of the ‘human organism’ – what we have in common with, and what makes us (mostly) distinct from, other forms of ‘organism’ – should now be clear. A rudimentary understanding of physiology and neurobiology can thus be very useful in helping to understand problems that occur at the levels of human individuals, groups of individuals, and larger scale groups of groups.

2.12 Conclusion: Sketching an Abstract Machine

This chapter has considered organisms both in the specific sense of the human organism, and in a more general sense, of ‘the organism’ as a particular form of ‘abstract machine’. From the more general perspective, the ‘the human organism’ represents just one out of many possible instantiations.

To what extent could ‘the organism as abstract machine’ – a particular, scientific way of thinking about types of relationship between types of elements – be usefully extended beyond the realm of biological phenomena, and used to gain insight into sociological phenomena? Based on the above, here is a preliminary, and highly speculative, list of defining qualities of the organism-as-abstract-machine:

  1. A salient and meaningful definition of a boundary, demarcating an ‘inside’ from an ‘outside’, which does predictive work in allowing observers to infer systematic differentials between the conditions on either side of the boundary with respect to a number of specifiable properties.
  2. Organisms may be of markedly differing sizes, but are composed of (for the purposes of analysis) irreducible elements known as ‘cells’.
  3. The overall purpose of an organism is homeostasis: the maintenance of the boundary defining inside from outside, and thus the establishment of an obdurate coherence in structure.
  4. As the size of (the number of cells contained within) the organism increases, so there is a tendency for two concurrent changes to occur with respect to the relationship between, and the performances of, individual cells: Firstly, for the degree of organism centralisation – the extent to which experiences encountered by aspects of the organism affect the entire organism – to increase; and secondly, for increasing functional specialisation of individual cells, with a cell’s particular function determined primarily by its relationship with other cells, and with the organism boundary. These two concurrent properties may be thought heuristically as defining the organism’s complexity.
  5. Within more complex organisms, centralisation allows more rapid information transfer between cells throughout the organism, and thus greater co-ordination of the organism as a whole. With centralisation, an internal command-and-control structure emerges. As the possible variety of specialised functions undertaken by any particular cell within an organism increases, so some cells (in the biological organism, those in the brain/central nervous system) take on the exclusive task of information processing, and others information transfer. However, the information reaching these central control cells is heavily filtered and mediated by intermediate cells, and only an abstracted fraction of the information available to the ‘source’ cells.
  6. Within more complex organisms, groups of cells may operate (and be understood more effectively) as organism sub-systems; each cell within the cluster may well exhibit relatively simple behaviour, but collectively exhibit more complex behaviour whose internal and external influences are better understood by thinking of the cluster as a single unit with a given set of properties, than by considering each constituent cell individually.
  7. The vast majority of cells (and sub-systems) tend to exhibit both ‘autonomous’ and ‘directed’ modes of operation. When adjacent cells (or sub-systems) are not signalling (transferring relevant information) that a particular cell (or sub-system) should do otherwise, the cell (or sub-system) has a default form of operation. When particular signals are received, however, the operations performed change in a systematic manner.

This list is by no means exhaustive or systematic, but hopefully highlights which types of element and relationship need to be identified in non-biological phenomena in order that the abstracted ontology of ‘the organism’ can be usefully applied.

Just as this chapter has considered the unintuitively small, so the next chapter will consider the unintuitively large: processes of societal emergence and change that played out over large sections of the globe, and over thousands of years.

## 2.1 Introduction

The topics covered within this chapter are perhaps the least intuitive of those covered within the entire thesis, and as such this chapter may be more challenging than many of those that follow. Paradoxically, this is because the contents of this chapter include the topic of intuition itself: what it is and how it emerged. This is because having intuitions -- as well as feelings, suspicions, beliefs, thoughts, and habits -- is part of what it means to be members of a particular type of complex organism (human beings), and this chapter is about organisms, both in the more specific sense of the human organism, and in a more general sense. It considers the ontology, the essential features and qualities, of 'the organism' as a particular way of thinking about structures and relationships between elements. The purposes of this chapter include:

1. To highlight the extent to which conscious acts such as thinking, seeing, imagining, and deciding are related to, and dependent upon, unconscious acts such as breathing, sensing, feeling, and responding.
2. To suggest the mutually interdependent relationship between 'higher' mental functions (those of which we are conscious) and 'lower' mental functions (those of which we are not conscious), and thus to make the case that, in order to understand the operation of 'higher' functions, 'lower' functions must also be understood.
3. Following from the previous point, to illustrate the importance of emotional responses in decision-making; and conversely, to present the theory that aspects of the brain and body function as emotional 'simulators' (or emotional-stimulus replicators).
4. To suggest some adaptive benefits, and thus the selective forces, that may lead to the development of 'higher' mental functions.
5. To illustrate the extent to which the majority of individuals' behaviours, actions, and performances are automated and unconscious, rather than consciously evaluated and pre-selected; as a corollary, to indicate the extent to which consciousness -- and choice-making (a conscious act) -- may be thought of, from the perspective of the organism, as a 'burden' to be avoided where possible, rather than as something to be 'sought' or 'maximised'.
6. To present a conceptual distinction, and description of the relationship, between the 'organism' and the 'environment'; and between the 'subject' and the 'object'.

In order to avoid having to do the additional work of developing a conceptual and terminological lexicon, as well as learning a new set of theories, this discussion will be anchored around the theories and descriptions provided by a single theorist, the neuroscientist Antonio Damasio, and on accounts presented within his book, *The Feeling of What Happens*.[^4]

[^4]: Damasio, A. R. (2000). *The feeling of what happens: body and emotion in the making of consciousness.* London, W. Heinemann.

By detailing the essential qualities of organisms, I will present not just a relatively novel description of social beings, but also a set of concepts and associated terminology, derived from analysis of one kind of emergent phenomena, which may be of use in later chapters when attempting to understand other phenomena. In particular, within later chapters I will attempt to identify similarities between the functioning of (very-small scale) biological organisms and (large scale) bureaucratic organisations. Illustrative questions include:

> To what extent do legal documents perform similar tasks within organisations as emotions do within organisms?

> To what extent do organisational roles and management hierarchies have similar information-processing qualities as those observed within organisms?

> To what extent do both organisms and organisations 'learn', and become 'habituated' to respond to particular forms of (external) environmental stimuli in the same ways?

> To what extent are organisations like organisms in their quest for self-replication and self-preservation?

## 2.2 Health Inequalities and Human Physiology

To begin with, consider the following description of the 'psychosocial model' of health inequalities:

> The original ideas behind [the psychosocial model] were based on the existence of two types of response to external threats shared by both humans and other animals. ... The most well known ... is the so called 'fight or flight' response. The body received alarm messages from the brain that can be thought of as activating one or other of two 'response circuits' [...]
>
> [The sympathetic-adrenomedullary circuit] involves the sympathetic nervous system, which, in balance with the parasympathetic nervous system, is responsible for much of what goes on in a routine way in the body without conscious awareness. They govern how fast the heart is beating, response to changes in outside temperature (shivering, sweating) and the pH (acidity) level of the blood, for example. Sympathetic nervous stimulation releases adrenaline from the adrenal medulla, and at the same time the ends of the sympathetic nerves themselves release noradrenaline. This prompts the release into the bloodstream of fibrinogen, a substance that helps blood to clot more easily. At the same time, the heart rate and blood pressure rise, and the small blood vessels (capillaries) nearest to the skin narrow sharply. This would prevent excessive blood loss from a wound, but it also has the effect of raising the blood pressure.[^5]

[^5]: Bartley, M. (2004). *Health Inequality: an introduction to theories, concepts, and methods.* Cambridge, Polity Press. pp. 80-1

To use terms and concepts which will be described in more detail later in the chapter, one could say the following: the release of fibrinogen, the increase in heart rate and blood pressure, the narrowing of capillaries -- are all automatic, unconscious components of what Damasio calls "complex, stereotyped patterns of response",[^6] known collectively as an 'emotion' -- and are the chronic, proximate causes of heart diseases which exhibit a well observed and consistent social gradient. One can treat such proximate causes -- for example, through the consumption of aspirin, which thins the blood -- in order to counter the 'abnormal', physiological imbalance of the human organism. However, following the logic of the psychosocial hypothesis, this 'abnormality', within the blood of the organism, results directly from a normal response to an abnormal environment.

[^6]: Damasio, A. R. (2000). *The feeling of what happens: body and emotion in the making of consciousness.* London, W. Heinemann. p. 55

From the perspective to be developed within this chapter, the above can be understood by thinking about the relationship between a biological organism and an aspect of the environment inhabited by the organism; for reasons to be made clear, we can refer to 'the aspect of the environment' as an 'object', and name this particular type of object a 'Stressor'.

The biological organism is in this case a 'human being', one of 'us' at the most fundamental physiological, emotional, cognitive and existential level. We would thus lose something of the substantive implications of this perspective if we were to refer to this organism in a way which implies an ontological 'otherness' ('they', 'it'). Instead, as 'we' are 'the organism', the above passage, a technical description of human physiology, begins to assume a tragic quality: We are made conscious of our emotional responses to different social situations, in order that we can make decisions about how to act in order to move away from the negative environmental aspects -- 'Stressors', in this case -- and towards positive environmental aspects. However, we exist in a social environment where such Stressors are all-pervasive. Our awareness of our emotions means that we know we should move away from the Stressors, but the environment is such that we cannot.

The use of the first-person plural within the above discussion is intended to suggest both what may be at stake, and what might be gained, from temporarily adopting an organism-centred perspective to social and societal issues, as opposed to the more intuitive individual- and group-centred perspectives we are likely to adopt in every-day life. Once the behaviours and sensations of the individual, and the sense of being an individual, are understood to be largely explicable in terms of, and often appear consequences of, the properties and functions of the organism, then the extent to which being able to make a gestalt shift in perspective, from that of the individual, to that of the organism, can aid our understanding of social, sociological, and societal phenomena becomes clearer.

## 2.3 The Individual and the Organism

To help make this transition, I will briefly consider an apparently esoteric and abstract problem usually addressed only by philosophers: the nature (and the notion) of 'self'. In Bertrand Russell's *History of Western Philosophy*, when discussing the French philosopher Rene Descartes' famous assertion "Cogito, ergo sum" ("I think, therefore I am"), he suggests the statement is problematic because 'I' (the '-o' part of 'cogito') is undefined. Instead, suggests Russell, the statement should simply assert that "there are thoughts".[^7] William James, a few decades earlier, made a similar observation within his book *The Principles of Psychology*.[^8]

[^7]: Russell wrote that:

    > Descartes's indubitable facts are his own thoughts -- using 'thought' in the widest possible sense. 'I think' is his ultimate premise. Here the word 'I' is really illegitimate; he ought to state his ultimate premise in the form 'there are thoughts'. The word 'I' is grammatically convenient, but does not describe a datum.

    Russell, B. (2000 [1946]). *History of Western Philosophy.* London, Routledge. p. 550

[^8]: James, W. (1890). *The Principles of Psychology.* New York, Dover. pp. 224-5

> It is astonishing what havoc is wrought in psychology by admitting at the outset apparently innocent suppositions that nevertheless contain a flaw. The bad consequences develop themselves later on, and are irredeemable, being woven through the whole texture of the work. ... The only thing which psychology has a right to postulate at the outset is the fact of thinking itself, and that must first be taken up and analyzed. [...]
>
> The first fact for us, then, as psychologists, is that thinking of some sort goes on. ... If we could say in English 'it thinks,' as we say 'it rains' or 'it blows,' we should be stating the fact most simply and with the minimum of assumption. As we cannot, we must simply that that thought goes on.

If accepting the concept of self is more problematic than accepting the concept of thinking, then this raises a new question: What is it that is doing the thinking? The problem of what is thinking led Damasio to suggest the following:

> How do we ever know that we are seeing a given object? How do we become conscious in the full sense of the word? How is the sense of self in the act of knowing implanted in the mind? The way into a possible answer for the questions on self came only after I began seeing the problem of consciousness in terms of two key players, the organism and the object, and in terms of the relationships those players hold in the course of their natural interactions. The organism in question is that within which consciousness occurs; the object in question is any object that gets to be known in the consciousness process; and the relationships between organism and object are the contents of the knowledge we call consciousness. Seen in this perspective, consciousness consists of constructing knowledge about two facts: that the organism is involved in relation to some object, and that the object in the relation causes a change in the organism.[^9]

[^9]: Damasio, A. R. (2000). *The feeling of what happens: body and emotion in the making of consciousness.* London, W. Heinemann. pp. 19-20

When a change occurs within an organism, as a result of its relationship with a particular object, Damasio suggests the organism experiences an 'image' of the object. In this case, he uses the term 'image' as a synonym for 'mental pattern',[^10] and by this usage an 'image' generalises beyond visual patterns, referring equally, for example, to tactile patterns (the sensation of being touched by a feather, or of a spider crawling along one's skin), auditory patterns (the sound of a police siren speeding away on a nearby street), olfactory patterns (the smell of roses or fetid gorgonzola), or any other form of sensory pattern.

[^10]: Ibid. p. 317

The organism does not have to be conscious of an image, or even be capable of consciousness, in order to experience the image, because an image of an object is simply the organism's subjective response to the object. (Therefore, if there were no organisms, there would be no objects, and no images.) There are some images that the organism is conscious of experiencing, and others that the organism is not. Whether subjectively experienced or not, biological correlates of images may -- in theory, but not always in practice -- be observed by third parties. Examples of such correlates include: changes in the skin's electrical conductance; changes in the form -- pattern, shape, intensity, duration -- of electromagnetic field surrounding the skull. From Damasio's perspective, all subjective mental images could, in theory, be shown to be perfectly correlated with a given objective pattern of biological activity.

## 2.4 Organisms and Boundaries

The term 'organism' itself is perhaps even more difficult, but at least as important, to understand. Most fundamentally, the concept of an 'organism' (much like that of an 'organisation', covered later in the thesis), is dependent upon "the definition of their boundary, the separation between that is in and what is out."[^11]

[^11]: Damasio, A. R. (1995). *Descartes' error: emotion, reason and the human brain.* New York, Avon Books. pp. 135-6

> The structure of the organism is inside the boundary and the life of the organism is defined by the maintenance of internal states within the boundary. Singular individuality depends on the boundary.[^12]

[^12]: Ibid., pp. 135-6

Damasio then states that "even when large variations occur in the environment that surrounds an organism, there is a dispositional arrangement in the organism's structure that modifies the inner workings of the organism."[^13]

[^13]: Ibid., pp. 135-6

> The dispositional arrangement ensures that the environmental variations do not cause a correspondingly large and excessive variation of activity within. When variations that trespass into a dangerous range are about to occur, they can be averted by some preemptive action; and where dangerous variations have already occurred, they can still be corrected by some appropriate action.[^14]

[^14]: Damasio, A. R. (2000). *The feeling of what happens: body and emotion in the making of consciousness.* London, W. Heinemann. pp. 135-6

The process by which a living system or organism comes to maintain its non-equilibrium stable state is called homeostasis. As Damasio explains:

> Homeostasis refers to the coordinated and largely automated physiological reactions required to maintain stable internal states in a living organism. Homeostasis describes the automatic regulation of temperature, oxygen concentration, or pH in your body.[^15]

[^15]: Damasio, A. R. (1995). *Descartes' error: emotion, reason and the human brain.* New York, Avon Books., pp. 40-41

From this perspective, organisms are thus fundamentally a form of homeostatic system, which use a series of internal feedback loops to self-regulate and maintain a non-equilibrium stable state with respect to a variety of qualities that exist within the organism. Once the internal structure of the organism loses its functional integrity, and can no longer maintain the differential boundary between itself and that which surrounds it, then, by this definition, it ceases to be alive.

## 2.5 Organisms as Abstract Machines

In asserting that the organism functions as a homeostatic system, the claim is implicitly being made that the organism represents a particular form of -- to use a term Manuel De Landa attributes to Giles Deleuze and Felix Guattari -- 'abstract machine.'[^16] This may be thought of, heuristically, as a formal specification of a set of relationships between components, but not of the materials out of which the components are constructed. For example, both an unopened bottle of wine and an unopened tin can may be thought of as different instantiations of the same abstract machine 'closed container', which defines a relationship -- that may be formally specified topologically -- between three components: 'contents', 'boundary', and 'outside'. These abstract machine components may be thought to 'exist' to the extent that one can perform isomorphic 'mapping' between substrates: 'the glass is to the bottle as the tin is to the can', 'the wine is to the bottle as the baked beans are to the can', and so on. The names given to enterprises which attempt to describe and understand this form of abstract machine, the homeostatic system, include 'systems theory', 'control theory' and 'cybernetics'.

[^16]: See De Landa, M. (2000). *A Thousand Years of Nonlinear History.* New York, Swerve. esp. pp. 57-70

To recapitulate: from a cybernetics perspective, a system could be considered to be 'alive' if it manages, through feedback loops and mechanisms within its internal structure, to maintain a non-equilibrium stable state, with regard to a set of definable and measurable properties, and with respect to that which is external to it (the environment). The 'equilibrium' refers to the normal state of the environment with respect to those properties by which the 'living system' and environment differ. Functionally, 'the boundary', by which the living system or organism is defined, refers to a significant and systematic difference between the conditions of a given range of properties -- moisture content, pH levels, electrical activity and so on -- between that which is outside (the environment) and that which is inside (the organism).

## 2.6 Objects and Emotions

An object is an aspect of the environment that has been differentiated from others by an organism. Even without consciousness, the organism can objectify aspects of the environment by producing systematically different responses to different environmental aspects (i.e. produce images of the objects). For example, towards some aspects of the environment, the organism may shrink, and to others it may swell. In this example the former environmental aspect would be, from the perspective of the organism, a 'shrink-inducing' object, whereas the latter would be a 'swell-inducing' object.

At the most basic level, an emotion is a response to an object. Within the hypothesised organism above, shrinking and swelling are, effectively, emotions. Damasio asserts that "emotions are part and parcel of the regulations we call homeostasis."[^17] In the Shrink-Sweller, one may presume, the emotional responses of shrinking and swelling may allow better regulation of core temperature when the external temperature of the environment decrease, in the cases of shrinking, or increases rapidly, in the case of swelling.

[^17]: Damasio, A. R. (2000). *The feeling of what happens: body and emotion in the making of consciousness.* London, W. Heinemann., p. 41

## 2.7 Feelings and Self

Decision-making requires a conscious, reasoning self. Damasio suggests the faculties for reason and consciousness emerge atop a biological hierarchy of increasingly complex biological and regulatory processes, as shown in @fig-levels-of-life-regulation below.

![Levels of Life Regulation (Redrawn from Damasio 2000, p. 55)](images/fig-021.png){#fig-levels-of-life-regulation}

Note that the arrows of causal influence, from highest to lowest level, run in both directions. In order for an emotion to become a feeling, there needs to exist the biological machinery for signalling, in one part of the organism, that an emotion is occurring in another part. Such signals can then be relayed to the non-emotive part of the organism, and the appropriate emotional response generated.

To continue with the example of the Shrink-Sweller, a shrink-inducing object may induce an emotional response through direct contact with one side of the organism. This change in the internal state of that side of the organism could then, through a sequence of internal causal influence, lead to a similar emotional response within the rest of the organism. If the sequence of internal causal influence is systematic in its behaviour in response to the change in the emotional state within an aspect of the organism, then this causal sequence functions as a signalling mechanism within the organism. This internal signalling, in response to emotional change, is the core quality of feeling. This particular pattern of signalling responses, resulting from the organism's contact with the object, is what Damasio refers to as an image; the Shrink-Sweller organism thus has an 'image' of shrink-inducing objects, and an 'image' of swell-inducing objects.

## 2.8 Centralisation and the Proto-self

An early prerequisite for the sense of self is the emergence of a complex, holistic system of emotional identification and signalling within the organism. This is the function of the central nervous system. With internal sensory centralisation, the emotional responses -- complex, unconscious behaviours -- of the entire organism can come to be coordinated, and where such coordination of activity is evolutionary advantageous such trends towards sensory centralisation develop. The sense of self depends upon the capacity of the organism to behave coherently as a single entity, and respond systematically, effectively and in a coordinated fashion to changes in any of its aspects.

When an organism becomes centrally connected in this way, it becomes meaningful to consider the organism to be an object with respect to itself. (To recall, an 'object' has here been defined as something which may have systematic influence upon the organism. With a central nervous system, which relays changes in the emotional state of any aspect of the organism, the organism comes to yield a coherent, systematic, unified response upon itself.)

## 2.9 Emotions and Decision-making

Given the above discussion, I am now in a better position to start to understand the process of decision-making, and the relationship between decision-making and emotions. This relationship should not be underestimated. As Damasio states, "emotion is integral to the processes of reasoning and decision making, for worse and for better."[^18]

[^18]: Ibid., p. 41

From this perspective, the faculty of decision-making relates intimately to the prediction, generation, and evaluation of differing emotional states under a variety of hypothesised environmental circumstances. The capacity to reason is thus ultimately dependent upon, and in service to, the capacity to know and compare feelings. The faculty for reason thus does not, and cannot, exist without emotion (which itself cannot exist without complex life). As Damasio states, "the engines of reason still require emotion, which means that the controlling power of reason is often modest."[^19]

[^19]: Ibid., p. 58

Thoughts, elements of the reasoning process, come to generate dispositional memories of how the organism, the biological predicate of the self, tends to respond to a given object.

> There is no such thing as pure perception of an object within a sensory channel ... They perceive an object ... the organism requires both specialized sensory signals and signals from the adjustment of the body, which are necessary for perception to occur. [...]
>
> The statement also holds true when you are simply thinking of an object rather than actually perceiving it in the world outside your organism. ... The records we hold of the objects and events that we once perceived include the motor adjustments we made to obtain the perception in the first place and also include the emotional reactions we had then. [Emphasis added]. [E]ven when we "merely" think about an object, we tend to reconstruct memories not just of a shape or color but also of the perceptual engagement the objects required and of the accompanying emotional reactions, regardless of how slight. ... [T]he images you form in your mind always signal to the organism its own engagement with the business of making images and evoke some emotional reactions. You simply cannot escape the affectation of your organism, motor and emotional most of all, that is part and parcel of having a mind.[^20]
>
> [...]
>
> Now let us turn to the case of an object that is not actually present but has, rather, been committed to memory. According to my framework, the memory of that object has been stored in dispositional form. Dispositions are records which are dormant and implicit rather than active and explicit, as images are. Those dispositional memories of an object that was once actually perceived include not only records of the sensory aspects of the object ... but also records of the motor adjustments that necessarily accompanies the gathering of the sensory signals; moreover the memories also contain records of the obligate emotional reaction to the object. As a consequence, when we recall an object, when we allow dispositions to make their implicit information explicit, we retrieve not just sensory data but also accompanying motor and emotional data. When we recall an object, we recall not just sensory characteristics of an actual object but the past reactions of the organism to that object.[^21]

[^20]: Ibid. pp. 147-8, emphasis in original unless otherwise stated

[^21]: Ibid. pp. 160-1, emphasis added

The image of the object is re-imagined, and through this the dispositional tendencies of the organism in response to the object come to be remembered (and thus to some extent involuntarily 'performed').

## 2.10 The As-if-body-loop

If mindfulness and consciousness perform, ultimately, the function of selecting from a limited range of potential behaviours, a limited proportion of the time, it follows that the vast majority of one's behaviours are not consciously determined at all. Instead, they exist as unconscious, predetermined patterns of behaviour, of varying levels of complexity, in response to particular 'stimuli'. If this process of remembering were to continually involve the entire body, the process could be expensive, in terms of both time and other resources (because, in this case, deciding would require rapid and substantial alteration of the body's electro-chemical and physiological functioning). Thus, in order to make the process of reasoning 'cheaper', faculties of the brain develop which simulate the responses of the rest of the body to a given object.

Thoughts entering this 'as-if-body-loop', as Damasio calls it, thus induce the same kinds of response within the periphery of the brain, from the perspective of the decision-making aspects of the brain, as would comparable signals from the brain to the rest of the body. Much of the brain thus behaves as a kind of emotional simulator, a cognitive laboratory under which various what-if scenarios, involving different relationships between objects, may be compared in order to assess their likely effects upon the organism.

## 2.11 From Memories and Dispositions to Habits and Habitus

The previous sections have described, from a neurological perspective, how decisions are made. In doing so, it has illustrated the extent to which unconscious processes pre-dominate both our responses to, and understanding of, our environment, and our conscious decisions to aspects thereof. Understanding individual and group behaviours may often be more a matter of understanding the decisions people don't make than the decisions that they do.

Recall from @fig-levels-of-life-regulation that the majority of processes performed by the human organism are below the level of consciousness, and also that the causal arrows -- from 'high' to 'low' processes -- go in both directions. This makes sense if one thinks about it: bring to one's mind and linger upon a vivid memory of a time one was scared, and within seconds this act of 'high reason' has permeated downwards through the levels, re-kindling a sense of the feeling of fear (initially just within the as-if-body loop), then inducing a genuine whole-body emotion, which in turn leads to changes in the performance of aspects of one's basic physiology (faster breathing, greater perspiration, and so on); the low level physiological change may then permeate back upwards, heightening the strength of the emotional response, which then amplifies the feeling (which now occurs throughout the entire body, rather than just within the as-if-body loop), which in turn makes the fearful memory even more vivid.

Conscious acts also enter the realm of the unconscious by generating dispositional tendencies within the organism; more colloquially, these may be termed 'habits', and may be thought of as complex patterns of behaviour acquired through experiences. Although the division is far from absolute, the capacity to acquire habits or dispositions is something that separates people from most other types of organism. As William James wrote:

> Man[^22] is born with a tendency to do more things than his has ready-made arrangements for in his nerve-centres. Most of the performances of other animals are automatic. But in him the number of them is so enormous, that most of them must be the fruit of painful study. If practice did not make perfect, nor habit economize the expense of nervous and muscular energy, he would therefore be in a sorry plight.[^23]

[^22]: The reader will hopefully forgive the archaic use of male nouns and pronouns to refer to both males and females.

[^23]: James, W. (1890). *The Principles of Psychology.* New York, Dover. p. 113

Being 'too conscious', therefore, of the vast and complex array of repertoires one needs to perform in order to function on a day-to-day basis, is likely to severely impede the extent to which one can perform such repertoires, and so function effectively. And as James then explained: "habit diminishes the conscious attention with which our acts are performed."[^24] He continues:

[^24]: Ibid. p. 114, emphasis in the original

> One may state this abstractly thus: If an act requires for its execution a chain, A, B, C, D, E, F, G, etc., of successive nervous events, then in the first performances of the action the conscious will must choose each of these events from a number of wrong alternatives that tend to present themselves; but habit soon brings it about that each event calls up its own appropriate successor without any alternative offering itself, and without any references to the conscious will, until at last the whole chain, A, B, C, D, E, F, G, rattles itself off as soon as A occurs, just as if A and the rest of the chain were fused into a continuous stream.[^25]

[^25]: Ibid. p. 114, emphasis added

As a tangible example of the above process, James offered the following illustration:

> The writer well remembers how, on revisiting Paris after ten years' absence, and, findings himself in the street in which for one winter he had attended school, he lost himself in a brown study, from which he was awakened by finding himself upon the stairs which led to the apartment in a house many streets away in which he had lived during that earlier time, and to which his steps from the school had then habitually led. We all of us have a definite routine manner of performing certain daily offices [...]. Our lower centres know the order of these movements, and show their knowledge by their 'surprise' if the objects are altered so as to oblige the movement to be made in a different way. But our higher thought-centres know hardly anything about the matter.[^26]

[^26]: Ibid., p. 115, emphasis added

Just as James' discussion of habits can be called upon to highlight the relationship between 'higher functions' of the human organism and every-day individual behaviours and experiences, so Pierre Bourdieu's concept of habitus can be used to link individual habits with social groups. As an editor's introduction to a volume of Bourdieu's essays describes the concept:

> The habitus is a set of dispositions which incline agents to act and react in certain ways. The dispositions generate practices, perceptions and attitudes which are 'regular' without being consciously co-ordinated or governed by any 'rule'. The dispositions which constitute the habitus are inculcated, structured, durable, generative and transposable -- features that each deserve a brief explanation. Dispositions are acquired through a gradual process of inculcation in which early childhood experiences are particularly important. Through a myriad of mundane processes of training and learning ... the individual acquires a set of dispositions which literally mould the body and become second nature. The dispositions produced thereby are also structured in the sense that they unavoidably reflect the social conditions within which they are acquired. ... [T]he similarities and differences that characterize the social conditions of existence of individuals will be reflected in the habitus, which may be relatively homogeneous across individuals from similar backgrounds. Structured dispositions are [...] ingrained in the body in such a way that they endure through the life history of the individual, operating in a way that is pre-conscious and hence not readily amenable to conscious reflection and modification [and] are generative and transposable in the sense that they are capable of generating a multiplicity of practices and perceptions in fields other than those in which they were originally acquired. As a durably installed set of dispositions, the habitus tends to generate practices and perceptions, works and appreciations, which concur with the conditions of existence of which the habitus is itself the product.[^27]

[^27]: Thompson, J. B. (1992). Editor's Introduction. *Language and Symbolic Power.* J. B. Thompson. Oxford, Polity Press: 1-31. pp. 12-13, emphasis in original.

In any case, the extent to which broader social factors systematically shape individual habits and dispositions, and the extent to which the capacity for persons to embody dispositions is dependent upon the qualities of the 'human organism' -- what we have in common with, and what makes us (mostly) distinct from, other forms of 'organism' -- should now be clear. A rudimentary understanding of physiology and neurobiology can thus be very useful in helping to understand problems that occur at the levels of human individuals, groups of individuals, and larger scale groups of groups.

## 2.12 Conclusion: Sketching an Abstract Machine

This chapter has considered organisms both in the specific sense of the human organism, and in a more general sense, of 'the organism' as a particular form of 'abstract machine'. From the more general perspective, the 'the human organism' represents just one out of many possible instantiations.

To what extent could 'the organism as abstract machine' -- a particular, scientific way of thinking about types of relationship between types of elements -- be usefully extended beyond the realm of biological phenomena, and used to gain insight into sociological phenomena? Based on the above, here is a preliminary, and highly speculative, list of defining qualities of the organism-as-abstract-machine:

1. A salient and meaningful definition of a boundary, demarcating an 'inside' from an 'outside', which does predictive work in allowing observers to infer systematic differentials between the conditions on either side of the boundary with respect to a number of specifiable properties.
2. Organisms may be of markedly differing sizes, but are composed of (for the purposes of analysis) irreducible elements known as 'cells'.
3. The overall purpose of an organism is homeostasis: the maintenance of the boundary defining inside from outside, and thus the establishment of an obdurate coherence in structure.
4. As the size of (the number of cells contained within) the organism increases, so there is a tendency for two concurrent changes to occur with respect to the relationship between, and the performances of, individual cells: Firstly, for the degree of organism centralisation -- the extent to which experiences encountered by aspects of the organism affect the entire organism -- to increase; and secondly, for increasing functional specialisation of individual cells, with a cell's particular function determined primarily by its relationship with other cells, and with the organism boundary. These two concurrent properties may be thought heuristically as defining the organism's complexity.
5. Within more complex organisms, centralisation allows more rapid information transfer between cells throughout the organism, and thus greater co-ordination of the organism as a whole. With centralisation, an internal command-and-control structure emerges. As the possible variety of specialised functions undertaken by any particular cell within an organism increases, so some cells (in the biological organism, those in the brain/central nervous system) take on the exclusive task of information processing, and others information transfer. However, the information reaching these central control cells is heavily filtered and mediated by intermediate cells, and only an abstracted fraction of the information available to the 'source' cells.
6. Within more complex organisms, groups of cells may operate (and be understood more effectively) as organism sub-systems; each cell within the cluster may well exhibit relatively simple behaviour, but collectively exhibit more complex behaviour whose internal and external influences are better understood by thinking of the cluster as a single unit with a given set of properties, than by considering each constituent cell individually.
7. The vast majority of cells (and sub-systems) tend to exhibit both 'autonomous' and 'directed' modes of operation. When adjacent cells (or sub-systems) are not signalling (transferring relevant information) that a particular cell (or sub-system) should do otherwise, the cell (or sub-system) has a default form of operation. When particular signals are received, however, the operations performed change in a systematic manner.

This list is by no means exhaustive or systematic, but hopefully highlights which types of element and relationship need to be identified in non-biological phenomena in order that the abstracted ontology of 'the organism' can be usefully applied.

Just as this chapter has considered the unintuitively small, so the next chapter will consider the unintuitively large: processes of societal emergence and change that played out over large sections of the globe, and over thousands of years.
