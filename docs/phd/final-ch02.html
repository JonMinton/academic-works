Chapter 2: Organisms, Decisions and Emotions: Some Theories of Very Small Scale Social Phenomena

Source: Pages 23–39 of MintonThesis.pdf (September 2009). Text extracted from PDF; figures extracted directly as images.

2 Organisms, Decisions and Emotions: Some Theories of Very Small Scale Social Phenomena 2.1 Introduction The topics covered within this chapter are perhaps the least intuitive of those covered within the entire thesis, and as such this chapter may be more challenging than many of those that follow. Paradoxically, this is because the contents of this chapter include the topic of intuition itself: what it is and how it emerged. This is because having intuitions – as well as feelings, suspicions, beliefs, thoughts, and habits – is part of what it means to be members of a particular type of complex organism (human beings), and this chapter is about organisms, both in the more specific sense of the human organism, and in a more general sense. It considers the ontology, the essential features and qualities, of „the organism‟ as a particular way of thinking about structures and relationships between elements. The purposes of this chapter include: 1. To highlight the extent to which conscious acts such as thinking, seeing, imagining, and deciding are related to, and dependent upon, unconscious acts such as breathing, sensing, feeling, and responding. 2. To suggest the mutually interdependent relationship between „higher‟ mental functions (those of which we are conscious) and „lower‟ mental functions (those of which we are not conscious), and thus to make the case that, in order to understand the operation of „higher‟ functions, „lower‟ functions must also be understood. 3. Following from the previous point, to illustrate the importance of emotional responses in decision-making; and conversely, to present the theory that aspects of the brain and body function as emotional „simulators‟ (or emotional-stimulus replicators). 4. To suggest some adaptive benefits, and thus the selective forces, that may lead to the development of „higher‟ mental functions. 5. To illustrate the extent to which the majority of individuals‟ behaviours, actions, and performances are automated and unconscious, rather than consciously evaluated and pre-selected; as a corollary, to indicate the extent to which consciousness – and choice-making (a conscious act) – may be thought of, from the perspective of the organism, as a „burden‟ to be avoided where possible, rather than as something to be „sought‟ or „maximised‟.

6. To present a conceptual distinction, and description of the relationship, between the „organism‟ and the „environment‟; and between the „subject‟ and the „object‟. In order to avoid having to do the additional work of developing a conceptual and terminological lexicon, as well as learning a new set of theories, this discussion will be anchored around the theories and descriptions provided by a single theorist, the neuroscientist Antonio Damasio, and on accounts presented within his book, The Feeling of What Happens4. By detailing the essential qualities of organisms, I will present not just a relatively novel description of social beings, but also a set of concepts and associated terminology, derived from analysis of one kind of emergent phenomena, which may be of use in later chapters when attempting to understand other phenomena. In particular, within later chapters I will attempt to identify similarities between the functioning of (very-small scale) biological organisms and (large scale) bureaucratic organisations. Illustrative questions include: To what extent do legal documents perform similar tasks within organisations as emotions do within organisms? To what extent do organisational roles and management hierarchies have similar information-processing qualities as those observed within organisms? To what extent do both organisms and organisations „learn‟, and become „habituated‟ to respond to particular forms of (external) environmental stimuli in the same ways? To what extent are organisations like organisms in their quest for self-replication and self-preservation?

One can treat such proximate causes – for example, through the consumption of aspirin, which thins the blood – in order to counter the „abnormal‟, physiological imbalance of the human organism. However, following the logic of the psychosocial hypothesis, this „abnormality‟, within the blood of the organism, results directly from a normal response to an abnormal environment. From the perspective to be developed within this chapter, the above can be understood by thinking about the relationship between a biological organism and an aspect of the environment inhabited by the organism; for reasons to be made clear, we can refer to „the aspect of the environment‟ as an „object‟, and name this particular type of object a „Stressor‟.

The biological organism is in this case a „human being‟, one of „us‟ at the most fundamental physiological, emotional, cognitive and existential level. We would thus lose something of the substantive implications of this perspective if we were to refer to this organism in a way which implies an ontological „otherness‟ („they‟, „it‟). Instead, as „we‟ are „the organism‟, the above passage, a technical description of human physiology, begins to assume a tragic quality: We are made conscious of our emotional responses to different social situations, in order that we can make decisions about how to act in order to move away from the negative environmental aspects – „Stressors‟, in this case – and towards positive environmental aspects. However, we exist in a social environment where such Stressors are all-pervasive. Our awareness of our emotions means that we know we should move away from the Stressors, but the environment is such that we cannot. The use of the first-person plural within the above discussion is intended to suggest both what may be at stake, and what might be gained, from temporarily adopting an organism-centred perspective to social and societal issues, as opposed to the more intuitive individual- and group-centred perspectives we are likely to adopt in every-day life. Once the behaviours and sensations of the individual, and the sense of being an individual, are understood to be largely explicable in terms of, and often appear consequences of, the properties and functions of the organism, then the extent to which being able to make a gestalt shift in perspective, from that of the individual, to that of the organism, can aid our understanding of social, sociological, and societal phenomena becomes clearer.

2.3 The Individual and the Organism To help make this transition, I will briefly consider an apparently esoteric and abstract problem usually addressed only by philosophers: the nature (and the notion) of „self‟. Instead, suggests Russell, the statement should simply assert that “there are thoughts”.7 William James, a few decades earlier, made a similar observation within his book The Principles of Psychology. 8

In this case, he uses the term „image‟ as a synonym for „mental pattern‟,10 and by this usage an „image‟ generalises beyond visual patterns, referring equally, for example, to tactile patterns (the sensation of being touched by a feather, or of a spider crawling along one‟s skin), auditory patterns (the sound of a police siren speeding away on a nearby street), olfactory patterns (the smell of roses or fetid gorgonzola), or any other form of sensory pattern. The organism does not have to be conscious of an image, or even be capable of consciousness, in order to experience the image, because an image of an object is simply the organism‟s subjective response to the object. (Therefore, if there were no organisms, there would be no objects, and no images.) There are some images that the organism is conscious of experiencing, and others that the organism is not. Whether subjectively experienced or not, biological correlates of images may – in theory, but not always in practice – be observed by third parties. Examples of such correlates include: changes in the skin‟s electrical conductance; changes in the form – pattern, shape, intensity, duration – of electromagnetic field surrounding the skull. From Damasio‟s perspective, all subjective mental images could, in theory, be shown to be perfectly correlated with a given objective pattern of biological activity.

2.4 Organisms and Boundaries The term „organism‟ itself is perhaps even more difficult, but at least as important, to understand. Most fundamentally, the concept of an „organism‟ (much like that of an „organisation‟, covered later in the thesis), is dependent upon “the definition of their boundary, the separation between that is in and what is out.”11 The structure of the organism is inside the boundary and the life of the organism is defined by the maintenance of internal states within the boundary. Singular individuality depends on the boundary.12 Damasio then states that “even when large variations occur in the environment that surrounds an organism, there is a dispositional arrangement in the organism‟s structure that modifies the inner workings of the organism.”13

The dispositional arrangement ensures that the environmental variations do not cause a correspondingly large and excessive variation of activity within. When variations that trespass into a dangerous range are about to occur, they can be averted by some preemptive action; and where dangerous variations have already occurred, they can still be corrected by some appropriate action.14 The process by which a living system or organism comes to maintain its nonequilibrium stable state is called homeostasis. As Damasio explains: Homeostasis refers to the coordinated and largely automated physiological reactions required to maintain stable internal states in a living organism. Homeostasis describes the automatic regulation of temperature, oxygen concentration, or pH in your body.15 From this perspective, organisms are thus fundamentally a form of homeostatic system, which use a series of internal feedback loops to self-regulate and maintain a nonequilibrium stable state with respect to a variety of qualities that exist within the organism. Once the internal structure of the organism loses its functional integrity, and can no longer maintain the differential boundary between itself and that which surrounds it, then, by this definition, it ceases to be alive.

2.5 Organisms as Abstract Machines In asserting that the organism functions as a homeostatic system, the claim is implicitly being made that the organism represents a particular form of – to use a term Manuel De Landa attributes to Giles Deleuze and Felix Guattari – „abstract machine.‟16 This may be thought of, heuristically, as a formal specification of a set of relationships between components, but not of the materials out of which the components are constructed. For example, both an unopened bottle of wine and an unopened tin can may be thought of as different instantiations of the same abstract machine „closed container‟, which defines a relationship –that may be formally specified topologically - between three components: „contents‟, „boundary‟, and „outside‟. These abstract machine components may be thought to „exist‟ to the extent that one can perform isomorphic „mapping‟ between substrates: „the glass is to the bottle as the tin is to the can‟, „the wine is to the bottle as 14

The names given to enterprises which attempt to describe and understand this form of abstract machine, the homeostatic system, include „systems theory‟, „control theory‟ and „cybernetics‟. To recapitulate: from a cybernetics perspective, a system could be considered to be „alive‟ if it manages, through feedback loops and mechanisms within its internal structure, to maintain a non-equilibrium stable state, with regard to a set of definable and measurable properties, and with respect to that which is external to it (the environment). The „equilibrium‟ refers to the normal state of the environment with respect to those properties by which the „living system‟ and environment differ. Functionally, „the boundary‟, by which the living system or organism is defined, refers to a significant and systematic difference between the conditions of a given range of properties - moisture content, pH levels, electrical activity and so on - between that which is outside (the environment) and that which is inside (the organism).

2.6 Objects and Emotions An object is an aspect of the environment that has been differentiated from others by an organism. Even without consciousness, the organism can objectify aspects of the environment by producing systematically different responses to different environmental aspects (i.e. produce images of the objects). For example, towards some aspects of the environment, the organism may shrink, and to others it may swell. In this example the former environmental aspect would be, from the perspective of the organism, a „shrinkinducing‟ object, whereas the latter would be a „swell-inducing‟ object. At the most basic level, an emotion is a response to an object. Within the hypothesised organism above, shrinking and swelling are, effectively, emotions. Damasio asserts that “emotions are part and parcel of the regulations we call homeostasis.”17 In the ShrinkSweller, one may presume, the emotional responses of shrinking and swelling may allow better regulation of core temperature when the external temperature of the environment decrease, in the cases of shrinking, or increases rapidly, in the case of swelling.

Note that the arrows of causal influence, from highest to lowest level, run in both directions. In order for an emotion to become a feeling, there needs to exist the biological machinery for signalling, in one part of the organism, that an emotion is occurring in another part. Such signals can then be relayed to the non-emotive part of the organism, and the appropriate emotional response generated. To continue with the example of the Shrink-Sweller, a shrink-inducing object may induce an emotional response through direct contact with one side of the organism. This change in the internal state of that side of the organism could then, through a sequence of internal causal influence, lead to a similar emotional response within the rest of the

organism. If the sequence of internal causal influence is systematic in its behaviour in response to the change in the emotional state within an aspect of the organism, then this causal sequence functions as a signalling mechanism within the organism. This internal signalling, in response to emotional change, is the core quality of feeling. This particular pattern of signalling responses, resulting from the organism‟s contact with the object, is what Damasio refers to as an image; the Shrink-Sweller organism thus has an „image‟ of shrink-inducing objects, and an „image‟ of swell-inducing objects.

2.8 Centralisation and the Proto-self An early prerequisite for the sense of self is the emergence of a complex, holistic system of emotional identification and signalling within the organism. This is the function of the central nervous system. With internal sensory centralisation, the emotional responses – complex, unconscious behaviours – of the entire organism can come to be coordinated, and where such coordination of activity is evolutionary advantageous such trends towards sensory centralisation develop. The sense of self depends upon the capacity of the organism to behave coherently as a single entity, and respond systematically, effectively and in a coordinated fashion to changes in any of its aspects. When an organism becomes centrally connected in this way, it becomes meaningful to consider the organism to be an object with respect to itself. (To recall, an „object‟ has here been defined as something which may have systematic influence upon the organism. With a central nervous system, which relays changes in the emotional state of any aspect of the organism, the organism comes to yield a coherent, systematic, unified response upon itself.)

2.9 Emotions and Decision-making Given the above discussion, I am now in a better position to start to understand the process of decision-making, and the relationship between decision-making and emotions. This relationship should not be underestimated. As Damasio states, “emotion is integral to the processes of reasoning and decision making, for worse and for better.”18 From this perspective, the faculty of decision-making relates intimately to the prediction, generation, and evaluation of differing emotional states under a variety of hypothesised environmental circumstances. 2.10 The As-if-body-loop If mindfulness and consciousness perform, ultimately, the function of selecting from a limited range of potential behaviours, a limited proportion of the time, it follows that the vast majority of one‟s behaviours are not consciously determined at all. Instead, they exist as unconscious, predetermined patterns of behaviour, of varying levels of complexity, in response to particular „stimuli‟. If this process of remembering were to continually involve the entire body, the process could be expensive, in terms of both time and other resources (because, in this case, deciding would require rapid and substantial alteration of the body‟s electro-chemical and physiological functioning). Thus, in order to make the process of reasoning „cheaper‟, faculties of the brain develop which simulate the responses of the rest of the body to a given object. Thoughts entering this „as-if-body-loop‟, as Damasio calls it, thus induce the same kinds of response within the periphery of the brain, from the perspective of the decisionmaking aspects of the brain, as would comparable signals from the brain to the rest of the body. Much of the brain thus behaves as a kind of emotional simulator, a cognitive laboratory under which various what-if scenarios, involving different relationships between objects, may be compared in order to assess their likely effects upon the organism.

2.11 From Memories and Dispositions to Habits and Habitus The previous sections have described, from a neurological perspective, how decisions are made. In doing so, it has illustrated the extent to which unconscious processes predominate both our responses to, and understanding of, our environment, and our conscious decisions to aspects thereof. Recall from Figure 2.1 that the majority of processes performed by the human organism are below the level of consciousness, and also that the causal arrows – from „high‟ to „low‟ processes – go in both directions. This makes sense if one thinks about it: bring to one‟s mind and linger upon a vivid memory of a time one was scared, and within seconds this act of „high reason‟ has permeated downwards through the levels, rekindling a sense of the feeling of fear (initially just within the as-if-body loop), then inducing a genuine whole-body emotion, which in turn leads to changes in the performance of aspects of one‟s basic physiology (faster breathing, greater perspiration, and so on); the low level physiological change may then permeate back upwards, heightening the strength of the emotional response, which then amplifies the feeling (which now occurs throughout the entire body, rather than just within the as-if-body loop), which in turn makes the fearful memory even more vivid. Conscious acts also enter the realm of the unconscious by generating dispositional tendencies within the organism; more colloquially, these may be termed „habits‟, and may be thought of as complex patterns of behaviour acquired through experiences. Although the division is far from absolute, the capacity to acquire habits or dispositions is something that separates people from most other types of organism. To what extent could „the organism as abstract machine‟ – a particular, scientific way of thinking about types of relationship between types of elements – be usefully extended beyond the realm of biological phenomena, and used to gain insight into sociological phenomena? Based on the above, here is a preliminary, and highly speculative, list of defining qualities of the organism-as-abstract-machine: 1. A salient and meaningful definition of a boundary, demarcating an „inside‟ from an „outside‟, which does predictive work in allowing observers to infer systematic differentials between the conditions on either side of the boundary with respect to a number of specifiable properties. 2. Organisms may be of markedly differing sizes, but are composed of (for the purposes of analysis) irreducible elements known as „cells‟. 3. The overall purpose of an organism is homeostasis: the maintenance of the boundary defining inside from outside, and thus the establishment of an obdurate coherence in structure. 4. As the size of (the number of cells contained within) the organism increases, so there is a tendency for two concurrent changes to occur with respect to the relationship between, and the performances of, individual cells: Firstly, for the degree of organism centralisation – the extent to which experiences encountered by aspects of the organism affect the entire organism – to increase; and secondly, for increasing functional specialisation of individual cells, with a cell‟s particular function determined primarily by its relationship with other cells, and with the organism boundary. These two concurrent properties may be thought heuristically as defining the organism‟s complexity. 5. Within more complex organisms, centralisation allows more rapid information transfer between cells throughout the organism, and thus greater co-ordination of the organism as a whole. With centralisation, an internal command-and-control structure emerges. As the possible variety of specialised functions undertaken by any particular cell within an organism increases, so some cells (in the biological organism, those in the brain/central nervous system) take on the exclusive task of information processing, and others information transfer. However, the information reaching these central control cells is heavily filtered

and mediated by intermediate cells, and only an abstracted fraction of the information available to the „source‟ cells. 6. Within more complex organisms, groups of cells may operate (and be understood more effectively) as organism sub-systems; each cell within the cluster may well exhibit relatively simple behaviour, but collectively exhibit more complex behaviour whose internal and external influences are better understood by thinking of the cluster as a single unit with a given set of properties, than by considering each constituent cell individually. 7. The vast majority of cells (and sub-systems) tend to exhibit both „autonomous‟ and „directed‟ modes of operation. When adjacent cells (or sub-systems) are not signalling (transferring relevant information) that a particular cell (or subsystem) should do otherwise, the cell (or sub-system) has a default form of operation. When particular signals are received, however, the operations performed change in a systematic manner. This list is by no means exhaustive or systematic, but hopefully highlights which types of element and relationship need to be identified in non-biological phenomena in order that the abstracted ontology of „the organism‟ can be usefully applied. Just as this chapter has considered the unintuitively small, so the next chapter will consider the unintuitively large: processes of societal emergence and change that played out over large sections of the globe, and over thousands of years.

2.1 Introduction
The topics covered within this chapter are perhaps the least intuitive of those covered
within the entire thesis, and as such this chapter may be more challenging than many of
those that follow. Paradoxically, this is because the contents of this chapter include the
topic of intuition itself: what it is and how it emerged. This is because having intuitions
-- as well as feelings, suspicions, beliefs, thoughts, and habits -- is part of what it means
to be members of a particular type of complex organism (human beings), and this
chapter is about organisms, both in the more specific sense of the human organism, and
in a more general sense. It considers the ontology, the essential features and qualities, of
„the organism‟ as a particular way of thinking about structures and relationships
between elements. The purposes of this chapter include:
1. To highlight the extent to which conscious acts such as thinking, seeing,
imagining, and deciding are related to, and dependent upon, unconscious acts
such as breathing, sensing, feeling, and responding.
2. To suggest the mutually interdependent relationship between „higher‟ mental
functions (those of which we are conscious) and „lower‟ mental functions
(those of which we are not conscious), and thus to make the case that, in order
to understand the operation of „higher‟ functions, „lower‟ functions must also
be understood.
3. Following from the previous point, to illustrate the importance of emotional
responses in decision-making; and conversely, to present the theory that aspects
of the brain and body function as emotional „simulators‟ (or emotional-stimulus
replicators).
4. To suggest some adaptive benefits, and thus the selective forces, that may lead
to the development of „higher‟ mental functions.
5. To illustrate the extent to which the majority of individuals‟ behaviours,
actions, and performances are automated and unconscious, rather than
consciously evaluated and pre-selected; as a corollary, to indicate the extent to
which consciousness -- and choice-making (a conscious act) -- may be thought
of, from the perspective of the organism, as a „burden‟ to be avoided where
possible, rather than as something to be „sought‟ or „maximised‟.

6. To present a conceptual distinction, and description of the relationship, between
the „organism‟ and the „environment‟; and between the „subject‟ and the
„object‟.
In order to avoid having to do the additional work of developing a conceptual and
terminological lexicon, as well as learning a new set of theories, this discussion will be
anchored around the theories and descriptions provided by a single theorist, the
neuroscientist Antonio Damasio, and on accounts presented within his book, The
Feeling of What Happens4.
By detailing the essential qualities of organisms, I will present not just a relatively novel
description of social beings, but also a set of concepts and associated terminology,
derived from analysis of one kind of emergent phenomena, which may be of use in later
chapters when attempting to understand other phenomena. In particular, within later
chapters I will attempt to identify similarities between the functioning of (very-small
scale) biological organisms and (large scale) bureaucratic organisations. Illustrative
questions include:
To what extent do legal documents perform similar tasks within organisations as
emotions do within organisms?
To what extent do organisational roles and management hierarchies have similar
information-processing qualities as those observed within organisms?
To what extent do both organisms and organisations „learn‟, and become
„habituated‟ to respond to particular forms of (external) environmental stimuli in
the same ways?
To what extent are organisations like organisms in their quest for self-replication
and self-preservation?

2.6 Objects and Emotions
An object is an aspect of the environment that has been differentiated from others by an
organism. Even without consciousness, the organism can objectify aspects of the
environment by producing systematically different responses to different environmental
aspects (i.e. produce images of the objects). For example, towards some aspects of the
environment, the organism may shrink, and to others it may swell. In this example the
former environmental aspect would be, from the perspective of the organism, a „shrinkinducing‟ object, whereas the latter would be a „swell-inducing‟ object.
At the most basic level, an emotion is a response to an object. Within the hypothesised
organism above, shrinking and swelling are, effectively, emotions. Damasio asserts that
"emotions are part and parcel of the regulations we call homeostasis."17 In the ShrinkSweller, one may presume, the emotional responses of shrinking and swelling may
allow better regulation of core temperature when the external temperature of the
environment decrease, in the cases of shrinking, or increases rapidly, in the case of
swelling.

2.8 Centralisation and the Proto-self
An early prerequisite for the sense of self is the emergence of a complex, holistic system
of emotional identification and signalling within the organism. This is the function of
the central nervous system. With internal sensory centralisation, the emotional
responses -- complex, unconscious behaviours -- of the entire organism can come to be
coordinated, and where such coordination of activity is evolutionary advantageous such
trends towards sensory centralisation develop. The sense of self depends upon the
capacity of the organism to behave coherently as a single entity, and respond
systematically, effectively and in a coordinated fashion to changes in any of its aspects.
When an organism becomes centrally connected in this way, it becomes meaningful to
consider the organism to be an object with respect to itself. (To recall, an „object‟ has
here been defined as something which may have systematic influence upon the
organism. With a central nervous system, which relays changes in the emotional state of
any aspect of the organism, the organism comes to yield a coherent, systematic, unified
response upon itself.)

2.10 The As-if-body-loop
If mindfulness and consciousness perform, ultimately, the function of selecting from a
limited range of potential behaviours, a limited proportion of the time, it follows that the
vast majority of one‟s behaviours are not consciously determined at all. Instead, they
exist as unconscious, predetermined patterns of behaviour, of varying levels of
complexity, in response to particular „stimuli‟. If this process of remembering were to
continually involve the entire body, the process could be expensive, in terms of both
time and other resources (because, in this case, deciding would require rapid and
substantial alteration of the body‟s electro-chemical and physiological functioning).
Thus, in order to make the process of reasoning „cheaper‟, faculties of the brain develop
which simulate the responses of the rest of the body to a given object.
Thoughts entering this „as-if-body-loop‟, as Damasio calls it, thus induce the same
kinds of response within the periphery of the brain, from the perspective of the decisionmaking aspects of the brain, as would comparable signals from the brain to the rest of
the body. Much of the brain thus behaves as a kind of emotional simulator, a cognitive
laboratory under which various what-if scenarios, involving different relationships
between objects, may be compared in order to assess their likely effects upon the
organism.

at last the whole chain, A, B, C, D, E, F, G, rattles itself off as soon as A occurs,
just as if A and the rest of the chain were fused into a continuous stream.25
As a tangible example of the above process, James offered the following illustration:
The writer well remembers how, on revisiting Paris after ten years‟ absence,
and, findings himself in the street in which for one winter he had attended
school, he lost himself in a brown study, from which he was awakened by
finding himself upon the stairs which led to the apartment in a house many
streets away in which he had lived during that earlier time, and to which his
steps from the school had then habitually led. We all of us have a definite
routine manner of performing certain daily offices [...]. Our lower centres know
the order of these movements, and show their knowledge by their „surprise‟ if
the objects are altered so as to oblige the movement to be made in a different
way. But our higher thought-centres know hardly anything about the matter.26
Just as James‟ discussion of habits can be called upon to highlight the relationship
between „higher functions‟ of the human organism and every-day individual behaviours
and experiences, so Pierre Bourdieu‟s concept of habitus can be used to link individual
habits with social groups. As an editor‟s introduction to an volume of Bourdieu‟s essays
describes the concept:

In any case, the extent to which broader social factors systematically shape individual
habits and dispositions, and the extent to which the capacity for persons to embody
dispositions is dependent upon the qualities of the „human organism‟ -- what we have in
common with, and what makes us (mostly) distinct from, other forms of „organism‟ --
should now be clear. A rudimentary understanding of physiology and neurobiology can
thus be very useful in helping to understand problems that occur at the levels of human
individuals, groups of individuals, and larger scale groups of groups.

2.12 Conclusion: Sketching an Abstract Machine
This chapter has considered organisms both in the specific sense of the human
organism, and in a more general sense, of „the organism‟ as a particular form of

„abstract machine‟. From the more general perspective, the „the human organism‟
represents just one out of many possible instantiations.
To what extent could „the organism as abstract machine‟ -- a particular, scientific way
of thinking about types of relationship between types of elements -- be usefully extended
beyond the realm of biological phenomena, and used to gain insight into sociological
phenomena? Based on the above, here is a preliminary, and highly speculative, list of
defining qualities of the organism-as-abstract-machine:
1. A salient and meaningful definition of a boundary, demarcating an „inside‟ from
an „outside‟, which does predictive work in allowing observers to infer
systematic differentials between the conditions on either side of the boundary
with respect to a number of specifiable properties.
2. Organisms may be of markedly differing sizes, but are composed of (for the
purposes of analysis) irreducible elements known as „cells‟.
3. The overall purpose of an organism is homeostasis: the maintenance of the
boundary defining inside from outside, and thus the establishment of an
obdurate coherence in structure.
4. As the size of (the number of cells contained within) the organism increases, so
there is a tendency for two concurrent changes to occur with respect to the
relationship between, and the performances of, individual cells: Firstly, for the
degree of organism centralisation -- the extent to which experiences encountered
by aspects of the organism affect the entire organism -- to increase; and
secondly, for increasing functional specialisation of individual cells, with a
cell‟s particular function determined primarily by its relationship with other
cells, and with the organism boundary. These two concurrent properties may be
thought heuristically as defining the organism‟s complexity.
5. Within more complex organisms, centralisation allows more rapid information
transfer between cells throughout the organism, and thus greater co-ordination of
the organism as a whole. With centralisation, an internal command-and-control
structure emerges. As the possible variety of specialised functions undertaken
by any particular cell within an organism increases, so some cells (in the
biological organism, those in the brain/central nervous system) take on the
exclusive task of information processing, and others information transfer.
However, the information reaching these central control cells is heavily filtered


and mediated by intermediate cells, and only an abstracted fraction of the
information available to the „source‟ cells.
6. Within more complex organisms, groups of cells may operate (and be
understood more effectively) as organism sub-systems; each cell within the
cluster may well exhibit relatively simple behaviour, but collectively exhibit
more complex behaviour whose internal and external influences are better
understood by thinking of the cluster as a single unit with a given set of
properties, than by considering each constituent cell individually.
7. The vast majority of cells (and sub-systems) tend to exhibit both „autonomous‟
and „directed‟ modes of operation. When adjacent cells (or sub-systems) are not
signalling (transferring relevant information) that a particular cell (or subsystem) should do otherwise, the cell (or sub-system) has a default form of
operation. When particular signals are received, however, the operations
performed change in a systematic manner.
This list is by no means exhaustive or systematic, but hopefully highlights which types
of element and relationship need to be identified in non-biological phenomena in order
that the abstracted ontology of „the organism‟ can be usefully applied.
Just as this chapter has considered the unintuitively small, so the next chapter will
consider the unintuitively large: processes of societal emergence and change that played
out over large sections of the globe, and over thousands of years.
