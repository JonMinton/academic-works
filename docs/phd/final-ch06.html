Chapter 6: Some Theories of Observable Social Phenomena and their Implications as Socioeconomic Mechanisms

Source: Pages 101–111 of MintonThesis.pdf (September 2009). Text extracted from PDF; figures extracted directly as images.

6 Some theories of observable social phenomena and their implications as socioeconomic mechanisms 6.1 Introduction The last chapter illustrated how, within large, bureaucratic structures, role definitions and metrics come to be relied upon increasingly by central nodes, as means of abstractly controlling and surveying the actions both of spatially and organisationally distant functionaries, and also of the broader world within which the bureaucratic structure operates. The reason for this is relatively simple: both spatial and cognitive factors operate to limit the number of people, places, and situations an individual can directly attend to;104 and within large, modern organisations the number of people and places connected via the bureaucratic structure tends to be much above this spatial and cognitive ceiling level. Although new technologies can do something to raise the ceiling level somewhat (increasing speed of travel and thus ‘compressing’ space and time to some extent; raising cognitive capacity slightly through the consumption of caffeine and other stimulant substances), the spatial and cognitive demands placed on managers of bureaucracies can to be vastly in excess of their capacities for direct knowledge and attention. Role-definitions and metrics are thus required as tools of indirect commanding and indirect knowledge (respectively), because direct commanding and knowing is no longer a possibility for all but the most proximal staff. Relying entirely on metrics, however, can produce a severely distorted picture of what functionaries’ actions are and ought to be, and of the world within which the bureaucracy operates. The bureaucracy only ‘sees’ what it measures, and only measures what it ‘sees’. Certain patterns of action and behaviour, whose function, existence, and importance is easily understood when experienced directly, can become either exaggerated or obscured when observed indirectly through an abstracted metric. The construction of metrics, therefore, can have a very profound and significant effect upon political actions and behaviours, and should thus be considered an important topic of investigation for social scientists interested in understanding political processes. Moreover, and as I will consider in more detail in the next chapter, the study of government metrics and standards can help to identify the meshwork of theoretical assumptions political actors, both past and present, have made and are making about

society and government, and so the „world‟ modern, bureaucratic political organisations are orientated towards recognising, responding to, bringing about or maintaining. Lurking within obscure technical documents and records are clues as to the broader „philosophy of society‟ and world view to which modern governments subscribe, and based upon which direct their actions and operations. Such a philosophy of society is likely not to have been consciously selected from a range of possible philosophies, but to have emerged from a combination of path dependency, function creep, enculturation and habituation, and hasty assumption-making following from the quotidian toils and demands of bureaucratic function. The overall purpose of this chapter is to briefly summarise a „superior story‟ told about quotidian practices, the kinds of things that happen all the time, and as a result perhaps tend to be noticed less than events more uncommon and salient. More specifically, the purposes of this chapter are as follows: 1. To introduce and describe a set of theories (and fairly well developed empirical research programme), developed by the social psychologist and anthropologist Alan Page Fiske, collectively referred to as „Relational Models Theory‟ (RMT). 2. To describe the each of the four constituent modes of social interaction – Communal Sharing, Authority Ranking, Equality Matching, and Market Pricing – that Fiske suggests can usefully be applied to understanding and typologising the vast majority of social behaviour. 3. To consider the extent to which these four modes of social interaction may usefully be considered from an economic perspective, by highlighting conceptual similarities between RMT and a relatively unpopular and uncommon school of economic thought known as Substantivism, most strongly associated with the economic historian Karl Polanyi. To do this, the parallels between the four modes posited in RMT and the four economic patterns suggested Polanyi‟s most famous book, The Great Transformation. I will make the argument that Substantivism is in some senses closer to the historical and conceptual origins of economics than much of the more atomistic and formalistic theory now associated with the discipline. 4. The chapter will conclude by suggesting how, from an RMT/Substantivist perspective, many economic metrics, relied upon by political bureaucracies to understand mass societal behaviour, systematically exaggerate the importance and prevalence of the fourth mode (Market Pricing) and attenuate the importance of the other three modes.

The broad aim of the chapter is to provide a framework for thinking about political philosophy and political economy as it actually appears to be currently applied. By doing this, the importance of government metrics in emphasising and de-emphasising, promoting and reprimanding, different forms and modes of interpersonal behaviour can be better understood. By thinking about modern political economic practice from the perspective of either Relational Model Theory or Substantivism (which I suggest at this level of detail are largely synonymous), it becomes clearer that many metrics and measures whose existence and political usefulness we take for granted, are in fact the result of a series of historical contingencies and „accidents‟, created in very different times and with very different functions in mind, that have through time grown into very powerful but unthinking political „actors‟, shaping and framing the decisions made by policy-makers throughout the world, by becoming integral parts of their conceptual landscape (their doxa). By offering RMT and Substantivism as attempts at formalising and foregrounding quotidian behaviours, their latent function as economic patterns can be made more apparent, and the need to link economic and political theory with everyday practice made much clearer.

6.2 Relational Models Theory According to Fiske, RMT “postulates that people in all cultures use just four relational models to generate most kinds of social interaction, evaluation and affect”; [p . 689] “that people‟s intentions with regard to other people are essentially sociable, and their social goals inherently relational: People interact with others in order to construct and participate in one or another of the four basic types of social relationships.” [p. 689] Although Fiske hypothesises that these four modes of social relations exist within all human cultures, he suggests that the domains of social action to which they are applied differ from culture to culture. [E]ach of the four universal models can be realized only in some culture-specific manner; there are no culture-free implementations of the models. Each model leaves open a number of parameters (variables or options) that require some determinant setting. The models cannot be operationalized without specifying application rules determining when and to whom and with regard to what they

apply, and without setting some parameters about how they are to be put into practice.105 Fiske suggests that almost all domains of human action, and types of personal relationship, are performed using not just one, but a complex combination of the relational modes: “For example, roommates may share tapes and records freely with one another (CS), work on a task at which one is an expert and imperiously directs the other (AR), divide equally the cost of gas on a trip (EM), and transfer a bicycle from one to the other for a price determined by its utility or exchange value (MP).”106 The degree to which a domain of human action is social (employs any of the modes) also varies between cultures, individuals, and encounters, at the limit producing what Fiske terms an „asocial‟ or „null relationship‟.107 The four modes – Communal Sharing, Authority Ranking, Equality Matching, and Market Pricing – are described below.

6.3 Mode 1: Communal Sharing (CS) Fiske defines communal sharing relationships as follows: Communal Sharing (CS) relationships are based on a conception of some bounded group of people as equivalent and undifferentiated. In this kind of relationship, the members of a group or dyad treat each other as all the same, focusing on commonalities and disregarding distinct individual identities. People in a CS relationship often think of themselves as sharing some common substance (e.g., “blood”), and hence think that it is natural to be relatively kind and altruistic to people of their own kind. Close kinship ties usually involve a major CS component, as does intensive love; ethnic and national identities and even minimal groups are more attenuated forms of CS. For two persons to be ready and willing to share with each other „communal‟ goods, both must recognise each other as „one of us‟. Within CS relationships, the social boundary separating „Us‟ from „Them‟ is usually diligently enforced (often at significant cost to group members), but, once someone is identified as „Us‟ rather than „Them‟, their individual debits from and credits to the communal resource pool are seldom monitored or scrutinised to any great detail. Instead, as one of „us‟, they are trusted to use those resources made available to them in an appropriate way.

6.4 Mode 2: Authority Ranking, and Mode 3: Equality Matching Fiske describes Authority Ranking in the following terms: Authority Ranking (AR) relationships are based on a model of asymmetry among people who are linearly ordered along some hierarchical social dimension. The salient social fact in an AR relationship is whether a person is above or below each other person. People higher in rank have prestige, prerogatives, and privileges that their inferiors lack, but subordinates are often entitled to protection and pastoral care. Authorities often control some aspects of their subordinates’ actions. People in an authority ranking relationship typically use spatial order and magnitude order metaphors to differentiate themselves: They think of “higher-ups” and speak of “belittling,” classify people into leaders in the forefront and followers behind, speak of superiors as “greater,” use plural nouns to address them, or accord them a larger personal space. Relationships between people of different ranks in the military are predominantly governed by this mode, as are relations across generations and between genders in many traditional societies. Conversely, Equality Matching relationships are described as follows: Equality matching (EM) relationships are based on a model of even balance and one-for-one correspondence, as in turn taking, egalitarian distributive justice, inkind reciprocity, tit-for-tat retaliation, eye-for-an-eye revenge, or compensation by equal replacement. People are primarily concerned about whether an EM

Ibid., p. 691

relationship is balanced, and keep track of how far out of balance it is. People in an EM relationship often mark their relationship with very concrete operations of balancing, comparing, or counting-out items in one-for-one correspondence. The idea is that each person is entitled to the same amount as each other person in the relationship, and that the direction and magnitude of an imbalance are meaningful. Common examples are people in a car pool or a baby-sitting cooperative, or the matching rules for competitive sports that require turn taking, equal time, and equal team size. Acquaintances and colleagues who are not intimate often interact on this basis: They know how far from equality they are, and what they would need to do to even things up. People can combine things at least entities of the same kind - to make up the difference. Equality matching is like a pan balance: people know how to assemble actions on one side to equal any given weight on the other side. To some extent, both AR and EM systems apply the same underlying principle: reciprocation. In this sense, AR may be considered a form of „vertical‟ reciprocal arrangement, whereas EM a form of „horizontal‟ reciprocal arrangement. Throughout chapters two and four, when considering both the emergence of polities more generally (chapter two) and the emergence of modern bureaucracies more specifically (chapter four), we came across countless examples of vertical social hierarchies emerging only as a result of an implicit (or even explicit) reciprocal agreement between Authority and Subordinate: Subordinates agree not to attempt to depose the Authority so long as the Authority agrees to keep the Subordinate safe from physical harm, through the establishment of a police force, and malnutrition, by maintaining and redistributing food and other supplies as and when needed (Chapter two). The Subordinate agrees to work for the Authority in Company X, and in exchange the Authority agrees to provide the Subordinate regular emoluments for his subservience, as well as access to a (subset of) the resources of Company X (Chapter four: in this case Authority Ranking relationships are combined with elements of Communal Sharing). Although the agreements struck between two agents may be unequal in terms of pay-off and prestige, and the inequity of the arrangements is often both a function of, and a means by which may be maintained, the existing disparity of power between agents, the reciprocal framework underlying AR relationships, as well as EM relationships, should not be forgotten. Charles Tilly implicitly makes this point when discussing the

emergence of what we think of as modern political rights (including Human Rights), over a period of around five-hundred years within Europe: [T]he significant thing about [political] rights is not that they should exist in the abstract, but that the state, rather than some other organization, should become the focus of their enforcement. Indeed, rather than thinking of abstract rights which are enforced (or violated) by states, it would be more illuminating to think of political rights as claims binding the agents of the state to specific groups of people: religious minorities can call on the state to defend them from persecution, adult citizens can insist the state give them their day in court, the indigent can claim sustenance from the state. These are political rights in a large sense – political in that they constitute binding claims on the agents of government, rather than on some other group.109 In each case, these political rights emerge as reciprocal, something-for-something agreements between agents, one of whom exists as (or claims itself to be, or retrospectively becomes known as) a polity or government. Such political agreements are not necessarily all AR relationships – consider, for example, bilateral trade agreements made between two states of equal size and wealth – but are overwhelmingly reciprocal in quality. In a more abstract and contentious sense, the principle of reciprocation may even be at the heart of effective and durable Communal Sharing relationships, as each individual member of a group with access to some Commons implicitly believes that allowing another group member access to the Commons now will mean they will have access to the Commons later. (This may be what Polanyi was intimating when he wrote that, “in the long run, all social obligations are reciprocal.”110)

6.5 Mode 4: Market Pricing Market pricing (MP) relationships are based on a model of proportionality in social relationships; people attend to ratios and rates. People in an MP relationship usually reduce all the relevance features and components under consideration to a single value or utility metric that allows the comparisons of many qualitatively and quantitatively diverse factors. Proportions (like intervals in EM) are continuous, and can take on any value. In an MP relationship, social value is defined by such ratios. In this kind of relationship, people tend to discuss (and probably cognize) value-relevant features of things propositionally, analyzing inputs and outputs using arbitrary symbols. Fiske suggests that the four models may have emerged sequentially in the following order – Communal Sharing, Authority Ranking, Equality Matching, Market Pricing111 – perhaps in step with increasing complexity and cognitive capacity of the organism and the social organisation within which it is embedded. As Fiske suggests, “The transformation of the relationship between a given pair of people or among the members of a particular group probably tends to move in the opposite direction, from MP to EM to CS, or from AR to CS”. As such, one may imagine human interpersonal relationships progressing along (or at least striving towards) a qualitative trajectory: beginning with the cold formality and calculating cost-benefit analyses of Market Pricing, and culminating with the warm intuitive informality, the relaxed and comforting sense that „what‟s mine is yours and what‟s yours is mine‟, and that one is part of something much greater than oneself, inherent in Communal Sharing relationships.

6.6 Substantivism As I stated at the start of this chapter, I believe that RMT represents something with resonances that extend beyond psychology or anthropology, and into economic theory and political economy. To highlight this, I want to draw attention to what appears to be a consonance between RMT and the Substantivist economic position sketched by economic historian Karl Polanyi within his 1957 book The Great Transformation112. Despite being separated by discipline, geography, and almost half a century, the two theorists make almost identical claims, about the existence of four basic modes of interpersonal relationship, and use terms that are almost synonyms of each others. 6.7 Conclusion: Bureaucracies, Metrics, and Modes By re-associating abstract economic theory with quotidian provisioning practices, we can hopefully gain a better sense of the broader purpose of political and economic decisions. If, even in the early twenty-first century, most real economic practices involve weaving a tapestry of interpersonal modes of interaction, then to what extent does attempting to maximise the amount of just one of the colours of weave – one of the relational modes – lead to the creation of a tapestry that is either richer or more durable? If one were to imagine each of the four relational modes as represented by a different colour (say red, yellow, green, and blue), then judging the „value‟ of the tapestry by the amount of thread of a single colour it contains, say red, does not appear to be a sensible activity (although, technically, it would be „rational‟). If such a comparative rule were to be applied to the creation of new tapestries, or the „improvement‟ of existing tapestries, then the result would be to attempt to create a new tapestry comprising only of the colour to be maximised, or to „improve‟ an existing tapestry either by surrounding it with a dense border of this colour, or to unpick existing threads in the „wrong‟ colours and replace them with threads of the „right‟ colour. Imagine, now, that one colour is easier to see, to measure, to record, than the others. In this case. Even though all colours are necessary for a rich tapestry, only reds appear a vibrant red; all other colours instead appear the same shade of drab, monochrome grey. Someone with this form of selective colour-blindness may well behave exactly as a robot applying the colour-maximising rule described above. Instead of seeing her actions as being a manifestation of a kind of absurd myopia, however, she would believe herself to be providing vibrancy and colour to something that before was dull and uninspiring. 115

Within CS relationships, social categories matter. For two persons to be ready and

6.4 Mode 2: Authority Ranking, and Mode 3: Equality Matching
Fiske describes Authority Ranking in the following terms:
Authority Ranking (AR) relationships are based on a model of asymmetry
among people who are linearly ordered along some hierarchical social
dimension. The salient social fact in an AR relationship is whether a person is
above or below each other person. People higher in rank have prestige,
prerogatives, and privileges that their inferiors lack, but subordinates are often
entitled to protection and pastoral care. Authorities often control some aspects of
their subordinates' actions. People in an authority ranking relationship typically
use spatial order and magnitude order metaphors to differentiate themselves:
They think of "higher-ups" and speak of "belittling," classify people into leaders
in the forefront and followers behind, speak of superiors as "greater," use plural
nouns to address them, or accord them a larger personal space. Relationships
between people of different ranks in the military are predominantly governed by
this mode, as are relations across generations and between genders in many
traditional societies.
Conversely, Equality Matching relationships are described as follows:
Equality matching (EM) relationships are based on a model of even balance and
one-for-one correspondence, as in turn taking, egalitarian distributive justice, inkind reciprocity, tit-for-tat retaliation, eye-for-an-eye revenge, or compensation
by equal replacement. People are primarily concerned about whether an EM

Ibid., p. 691


relationship is balanced, and keep track of how far out of balance it is. People in
an EM relationship often mark their relationship with very concrete operations
of balancing, comparing, or counting-out items in one-for-one correspondence.
The idea is that each person is entitled to the same amount as each other person
in the relationship, and that the direction and magnitude of an imbalance are
meaningful. Common examples are people in a car pool or a baby-sitting
cooperative, or the matching rules for competitive sports that require turn taking,
equal time, and equal team size. Acquaintances and colleagues who are not
intimate often interact on this basis: They know how far from equality they are,
and what they would need to do to even things up. People can combine things at least entities of the same kind - to make up the difference. Equality matching
is like a pan balance: people know how to assemble actions on one side to equal
any given weight on the other side.
To some extent, both AR and EM systems apply the same underlying principle:
reciprocation. In this sense, AR may be considered a form of „vertical‟ reciprocal
arrangement, whereas EM a form of „horizontal‟ reciprocal arrangement. Throughout
chapters two and four, when considering both the emergence of polities more generally
(chapter two) and the emergence of modern bureaucracies more specifically (chapter
four), we came across countless examples of vertical social hierarchies emerging only
as a result of an implicit (or even explicit) reciprocal agreement between Authority and
Subordinate: Subordinates agree not to attempt to depose the Authority so long as the
Authority agrees to keep the Subordinate safe from physical harm, through the
establishment of a police force, and malnutrition, by maintaining and redistributing food
and other supplies as and when needed (Chapter two). The Subordinate agrees to work
for the Authority in Company X, and in exchange the Authority agrees to provide the
Subordinate regular emoluments for his subservience, as well as access to a (subset of)
the resources of Company X (Chapter four: in this case Authority Ranking relationships
are combined with elements of Communal Sharing).
Although the agreements struck between two agents may be unequal in terms of pay-off
and prestige, and the inequity of the arrangements is often both a function of, and a
means by which may be maintained, the existing disparity of power between agents,
the reciprocal framework underlying AR relationships, as well as EM relationships,
should not be forgotten. Charles Tilly implicitly makes this point when discussing the


emergence of what we think of as modern political rights (including Human Rights),
over a period of around five-hundred years within Europe:
[T]he significant thing about [political] rights is not that they should exist in the
abstract, but that the state, rather than some other organization, should become
the focus of their enforcement. Indeed, rather than thinking of abstract rights
which are enforced (or violated) by states, it would be more illuminating to think
of political rights as claims binding the agents of the state to specific groups of
people: religious minorities can call on the state to defend them from
persecution, adult citizens can insist the state give them their day in court, the
indigent can claim sustenance from the state. These are political rights in a large
sense -- political in that they constitute binding claims on the agents of
government, rather than on some other group.109
In each case, these political rights emerge as reciprocal, something-for-something
agreements between agents, one of whom exists as (or claims itself to be, or
retrospectively becomes known as) a polity or government. Such political agreements
are not necessarily all AR relationships -- consider, for example, bilateral trade
agreements made between two states of equal size and wealth -- but are overwhelmingly
reciprocal in quality. In a more abstract and contentious sense, the principle of
reciprocation may even be at the heart of effective and durable Communal Sharing
relationships, as each individual member of a group with access to some Commons
implicitly believes that allowing another group member access to the Commons now
will mean they will have access to the Commons later. (This may be what Polanyi was
intimating when he wrote that, "in the long run, all social obligations are reciprocal."110)

6.5 Mode 4: Market Pricing
Market pricing (MP) relationships are based on a model of proportionality in
social relationships; people attend to ratios and rates. People in an MP
relationship usually reduce all the relevance features and components under
consideration to a single value or utility metric that allows the comparisons of
many qualitatively and quantitatively diverse factors. People organize their

prototypical of MP relations, but there are also many MP relationships that do

inputs and outputs using arbitrary symbols. For example, the use of numbers in
equations is common, and people use abstract, conceptually formulated analysis

Of the four modes of social interaction, Market Pricing appears the most cognitively
demanding and, to some extent, unintuitive. Fiske suggests that the four models may
have emerged sequentially in the following order -- Communal Sharing, Authority
Ranking, Equality Matching, Market Pricing111 -- perhaps in step with increasing
complexity and cognitive capacity of the organism and the social organisation within
which it is embedded. As Fiske suggests, "The transformation of the relationship
between a given pair of people or among the members of a particular group probably
tends to move in the opposite direction, from MP to EM to CS, or from AR to CS". As
such, one may imagine human interpersonal relationships progressing along (or at least
striving towards) a qualitative trajectory: beginning with the cold formality and
calculating cost-benefit analyses of Market Pricing, and culminating with the warm
intuitive informality, the relaxed and comforting sense that „what‟s mine is yours and
what‟s yours is mine‟, and that one is part of something much greater than oneself,
inherent in Communal Sharing relationships.

6.7 Conclusion: Bureaucracies, Metrics, and Modes
By re-associating abstract economic theory with quotidian provisioning practices, we
can hopefully gain a better sense of the broader purpose of political and economic
decisions. If, even in the early twenty-first century, most real economic practices
involve weaving a tapestry of interpersonal modes of interaction, then to what extent
does attempting to maximise the amount of just one of the colours of weave -- one of the
relational modes -- lead to the creation of a tapestry that is either richer or more durable?
If one were to imagine each of the four relational modes as represented by a different
colour (say red, yellow, green, and blue), then judging the „value‟ of the tapestry by the
amount of thread of a single colour it contains, say red, does not appear to be a sensible
activity (although, technically, it would be „rational‟). If such a comparative rule were to
be applied to the creation of new tapestries, or the „improvement‟ of existing tapestries,
then the result would be to attempt to create a new tapestry comprising only of the
colour to be maximised, or to „improve‟ an existing tapestry either by surrounding it
with a dense border of this colour, or to unpick existing threads in the „wrong‟ colours
and replace them with threads of the „right‟ colour.
Imagine, now, that one colour is easier to see, to measure, to record, than the others. In
this case. Even though all colours are necessary for a rich tapestry, only reds appear a
vibrant red; all other colours instead appear the same shade of drab, monochrome grey.
Someone with this form of selective colour-blindness may well behave exactly as a
robot applying the colour-maximising rule described above. Instead of seeing her
actions as being a manifestation of a kind of absurd myopia, however, she would
believe herself to be providing vibrancy and colour to something that before was dull
and uninspiring.
