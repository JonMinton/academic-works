Work and its Other: A Multiple-perspective Analysis of Incapacity Benefit Reform

Jonathan William Minton
Department of Sociology
University of York
July 2009

Abstract

This is a thesis of two halves: five ‘theory’ chapters and five ‘empirical’ chapters, prefaced by an introduction, and then followed by a conclusion.

The five ‘empirical’ chapters are based around understanding the development and roll-out of a programme of Incapacity Benefits reform known as ‘Pathways to Work’, which was first introduced in 2002. Pathways to Work has since been implemented in all Jobcentre Plus offices across the UK, and so this half of the thesis could be seen as a case study of a ‘successfully implemented’ government policy.

The five ‘theoretical’ chapters introduce, explain, and interpret a range of theories about social processes that operate at various temporal and spatial scales. These theories range from the very small scale (human neurobiology) to the very large scale (archaeology and biogeography), and conclude with mid-scale theories relating to observed and intuitive everyday interpersonal interactions.

The two halves of the thesis are intended to be complementary, with the ‘theoretical’ half illuminating the ‘empirical’ half and the ‘empirical’ half illuminating the ‘theoretical’ half. The thesis concludes by offering a number of policy suggestions, and making the complementarity between thesis halves more explicit.

Acknowledgements

Progress towards completing this thesis has been approximately sigmoid in shape: slow in the first year, increasingly fast in the second year (“I think you’re about six months from completion…”), and then slow again in the third year (“…maybe not.”). So slow, in fact, that the third year stretched over into a fourth year as, well, ‘other stuff happened’. Sigmoid curves are asymptotic, never quite reaching their destination. I want to apologise to my thesis advisory ‘team’ – Roger Burrows, Danny Dorling, and Kate Pickett – for at times making the sigmoid curve look more like prophecy than approximation. I also want to thank Roger and Danny for allowing me the opportunity to begin the PhD in the first place, and for then allowing it to develop (mutate?) from a GIS project to something closer to a work of applied political philosophy.

I would also like to thank Stuart Adam, Jenny Allen, Kelly Benneworth, Beverly Geesin, Roger Bowles, Jonathan Bradshaw, Gill Callaghan, Colin Campbell, Stephen Clayton, Thomas Cochrane, Jacqueline Davidson, Richard Dorsett, Paul Drew, Jean-Baptiste Gouyon, Steve Hall, Martin Ham, Nicola Holt, Gary King, Richard King, Graham Lewis, Daryl Martin, Duncan McVicar, Rob Mellows, Dan Mercea, Andrew Oswald, Theo Papadopoulos, Simon Parker, Nicholas Pleace, Darren Reed, Barbara Robertson, Roy Sainsbury, Simon Shaw, Phil Stanworth, Charles Tilly, Merran Toerien, Emma Uprichard, Roberto de Vogli, Simon Walker, Gemma Warren, Richard Wilkinson, Astrid Winkler, and Simon Winlow. Each of these people, I think, has contributed towards my thinking about the concepts, facts, and ideas developed over the course of the PhD. They, of course, deserve some of the credit but none of the blame for what is presented here, which they may disagree with entirely.

Finally, I would like to thank my mother, Helen Minton, for eagle-eyed proofreading, and to her and my father, Nigel Minton, for allowing me to soliloquise at length to them about many of the ideas contained within this thesis, even where such soliloquies were unsolicited; and to thank Natalie Wheatley, also for proof-reading, and for offering persistent encouragement that I “complete and submit it”, despite not really knowing what ‘it’ is.
