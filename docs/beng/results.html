Results Of System Using Single Test Case

Introduction

Here, the stages of the prototype system listed in section 3 are demonstrated using a single test case.

In the test case, the bounded area was a rectangle 70mm wide and 50 mm high. This was marked out using four black square blocks 10 mm wide. Black was used as the marker colour as it is a poor choice of marker colour (due to the reasons given in section 2.3.2), and so demonstrating that the system works with this colour scheme suggest that it should also work with others (where the affect of bright lights and shadows can be more easily mitigated).

The object is represented by a small movable red rectangle 8mm wide and 6 mm high. Am image of this object within the bounded area, at the ‘correct’ position, is taken, and loaded, as an archived image, into the system at the start of the program sequence:

Archive image of marker object (red rectangle) in correct position

[Fig. 4.2.1]

Note that, within the bounded area, other objects, of a different colour, also exist. To work, the prototype system should not be affected by the position of these other objects. The background is graph paper, which, as a repeating pattern, creates ‘noise’ when a DIC approach is used (due to localised areas of image correlation). Both of these factors make the test case good for demonstrating the rigorousness of the system.

Determining Rotation

It is now relatively simple to determine the plane-parallel rotation of the camera to the object plane. The process is as follows:

Compare the vertical distance between the two points on the left (Left half of image), with the two points on the right (Right half of image). The camera is closer to the side whose points are further apart. In the test case, the two points on the left are further apart.

The points from this half are more accurate representations of the centres of the markers, as the camera is closer to them, and thus their effective resolution in the image is higher. Due to the way the markers are formed, borderlines of a certain number of pixels are created which extend slightly beyond the boundaries of the marker in the original image (Fig. 2.3.10; Fig 2.3.11). The distortion of the marker shape and position due to these borderlines are greater if the marker is smaller; thus the larger (in this case closer) markers provide more accurate estimates of their central positions. These two position co-ordinates on the left side are considered thus those used to determine the angle.

The horizontal displacement between the two points is divided by the vertical displacement, and the tangent of this value if found to determine the angle between the two markers.

If the angle is small (less than one degree in magnitude, in the case of the prototype), then the program passes to the next stage. Otherwise, the angle between the two points is given to the user, together with instructions on what action to take. In the test case, the first input image yields and angle of –6.1 degrees (due to the way positive and negative displacement is defined, and the instruction:

Stage 2: Finding Object Disparity

Introduction

Now the camera has been positioned correctly, the prototype needs to determine how far the object (the red rectangle) is displaced from its position in the archive image. In all current (non-archive) images, the object is 6.0mm further to the right, and 4.0mm lower, than it is in the archive image.

In order to determine the disparity of the object, any disparity between the test and archive images as a whole – known as rigid body disparity – needs to be determined. Stage one of the process allows the angle of the camera to be corrected relative to the plane, but does not correct the position along the axes of the plane from which the camera views the bounded area. 

Once this rigid body disparity has been found, it can be compensated for when performing a digital image correlation (DIC), so that ‘like’ regions – regions of the images common to both archive and current – can be compared.

An object disparity value found by performing a DIC on the common regions of the pair of images, in pixels, then needs to be converted to a value in millimetres, by using a scale found by comparing real-life boundary marker geometries with those located in pixels.

Thus, stage two essentially involves three sub-stages, each of which will now be detailed for the test case in sequence.

Sub-stage 1: Rigid-body Disparity

Rigid body disparity, the disparity between two images due to a shift between the images as a whole (rather than displacement of a small-sub region of the image – i.e. the movable object), can be determined in a number of ways: 

If the images are of a flat surface with an amorphous (non-repeating) pattern on it – such as wood-chip wallpaper –, and where any movable objects within the image areas occupy only a small image area, and/or of a low intensity within the intensity plane used, then simply performing a DIC of both whole images should produce an output with the greatest intensity peak at the rigid-body displacement position.

There are a number of problems with this approach: A DIC of the whole image will be affected by both rigid body and object disparity, and may produce a results whose peak is at a position that is a ‘compromise’ between the rigid-body displacement (which should have the greatest ‘energy’ in the image, as most of the features of the image match at this disparity position), and the object displacement. Thus it would produce the wrong value for rigid-body disparity.

Also, most machine work surfaces do not have fixed amorphous patterns on them in a region of interest. Consider a work surface on a drilling machine: Originally, its work surface may be shiny and featureless; over time features on the surface may result in the form of scratches and general wear, but these features are likely to change over time - as more scratches are introduced -, and so cannot be used to determine rigid body disparity between two images taken – for instance – months apart. 

A better method, therefore, may be to use the boundary markers found in the previous stages. This should be more reliable, as they are strong, already identified features, which are not as subject to change in appearance over time than the region actually within the working area.

Thus, in the test case, the rigid-body disparity is found by comparing the boundary marker masks of the current and archive image, rather than the images themselves.

One can consider how accurate these values seem by comparing them with the actual object displacement: 6.0mm to the right, and 4.0mm downward. Due to the way Matlab stores images, the origin is taken as the top left corner, thus displacements to the right, and downward, are both positive displacements. Both the x and y object displacement values are positive, which means the recorded object displacement is in the correct direction. 

The ratio between x displacement and y displacement in both cases (actual in mm, and recorded in pixels) is 3/2 (15pixels/10pixels = 6mm/4mm = 3/2), again suggesting that the displacement value found is accurate.

The bounded object plane area was 70.0mm x 50.0mm, or 3500.0mm2; the object displacement figure found was accurate to ±0.5mm2 (The square root of 0.52 + 0.02). This gives a calculated accuracy level to within 0.5 / 3500, or 0.14 per mil. Using the 0.40 and 0.37 mm / pixel resolution figures calculated earlier gives a displacement determination accuracy of ±0.54mm2, the same value to 1 significant figure.

In two of the three cases, the system is able to identify both the markers and the movable object exclusively, without any difficulty. In the third image (Fig 4.3.3), part of a shadow formed by one of the non-critical objects is of a colour and intensity (black), and of a significant enough size, to be wrongly identified as part of a marker. This is a problem specific to greyscale (rather than colour) markers, and again highlights why colour marker colours are often used (Note that the object mask, of a vivid red colour, was successfully and exclusively identified in all three cases). Even this erroneously included portion of the feature could be successfully identified as erroneous by the computer and removed by, for instance, further erosion filtering, or simply (as, in the prototype case, there are just four markers) retaining only the largest object in each quarter of the mask. 

Where erroneously identified objects exist that are of similar or greater size to the correctly identified mask features, a more complicated system - that, for example, determines which of the feature objects identified in the mask is most likely to be the real one based on the known marker geometry – would have to be used.

As stated previously, using a greyscale marker represents a worst-case scenario regarding ease of identification. This shows in these results, where the shadow formed by one particular non-critical object, near the top right of the bounded area, is erroneously identified in almost every marker mark of the image with sub-optimal lighting. In the other case (Image 5), part of the edge of the image was overexposed, and incorrectly identified. The results of image 4 are important, as, in this case, the markers were easily identified. This type of image could be created by either increasing the contrast of the camera, or by switching on a portable light source behind the camera. This would have the effect of removing the effect of uneven lighting on creating erroneous feature identification (such as in image 5), and more easily than attempting to block light. Afterwards, the light source could be de-activated to allow the red object to be identified.

In a more general case, a strong light source that makes features of a certain colour more prominent could be used to make identifying that particular feature easier.

As one can see, as the distance increases, the ratio tends towards 1. This makes sense due to the effects a limited resolution has on the way an object is interpreted at different distances. As the object gets further away, more information about it is lost, and less can be interpreted about it. Even looking at the masks of the images, they start to appear more rectangular when smaller, and this effect is resulting in the increasingly inaccurate ratio results.

It would be possible to use the actual number of pixels between markers, and the size of markers in the images, to estimate the distance from the camera to the plane, and thus the accuracy of other figures such as estimated angle. 

In DIC, is important that the two images to be correlated are at the same distance (as well as angle). Thus, a final system would need to be able to determine and inform the user of its distance from the plane, as well as angle to it.

***Determining Rotation***

It is now relatively simple to determine the plane-parallel rotation of the camera to the object plane. The process is as follows:

Compare the vertical distance between the two points on the left (Left half of image), with the two points on the right (Right half of image). The camera is closer to the side whose points are further apart. In the test case, the two points on the left are further apart.

The points from this half are more accurate representations of the centres of the markers, as the camera is closer to them, and thus their effective resolution in the image is higher. Due to the way the markers are formed, borderlines of a certain number of pixels are created which extend slightly beyond the boundaries of the marker in the original image (Fig. 2.3.10; Fig 2.3.11). The distortion of the marker shape and position due to these borderlines are greater if the marker is smaller; thus the larger (in this case closer) markers provide more accurate estimates of their central positions. These two position co-ordinates on the left side are considered thus those used to determine the angle.

The horizontal displacement between the two points is divided by the vertical displacement, and the tangent of this value if found to determine the angle between the two markers.

If the angle is small (less than one degree in magnitude, in the case of the prototype), then the program passes to the next stage. Otherwise, the angle between the two points is given to the user, together with instructions on what action to take. ***Stage 2: Finding Object Disparity***

***Introduction***

Now the camera has been positioned correctly, the prototype needs to determine how far the object (the red rectangle) is displaced from its position in the archive image. In all current (non-archive) images, the object is 6.0mm further to the right, and 4.0mm lower, than it is in the archive image.

In order to determine the disparity of the object, any disparity between the test and archive images as a whole – known as rigid body disparity – needs to be determined. Stage one of the process allows the *angle* of the camera to be corrected relative to the plane, but does not correct the *position* along the axes of the plane from which the camera views the bounded area. 

Once this rigid body disparity has been found, it can be compensated for when performing a digital image correlation (DIC), so that ‘like’ regions – regions of the images common to both archive and current – can be compared.

An object disparity value found by performing a DIC on the common regions of the pair of images, in pixels, then needs to be converted to a value in millimetres, by using a scale found by comparing real-life boundary marker geometries with those located in pixels.

Thus, stage two essentially involves three sub-stages, each of which will now be detailed for the test case in sequence.

***Sub-stage 1: Rigid-body Disparity***

Rigid body disparity, the disparity between two images due to a shift between the images as a whole (rather than displacement of a small-sub region of the image – i.e. the movable object), can be determined in a number of ways: 

If the images are of a flat surface with an amorphous (non-repeating) pattern on it – such as wood-chip wallpaper –, and where any movable objects within the image areas occupy only a small image area, and/or of a low intensity within the intensity plane used, then simply performing a DIC of both whole images should produce an output with the greatest intensity peak at the rigid-body displacement position.

There are a number of problems with this approach: A DIC of the whole image will be affected by both rigid body and object disparity, and may produce a results whose peak is at a position that is a ‘compromise’ between the rigid-body displacement (which should have the greatest ‘energy’ in the image, as most of the features of the image match at this disparity position), and the object displacement. Thus it would produce the wrong value for rigid-body disparity.

Also, most machine work surfaces do not have fixed amorphous patterns on them in a region of interest. Consider a work surface on a drilling machine: Originally, its work surface may be shiny and featureless; over time features on the surface may result in the form of scratches and general wear, but these features are likely to change over time - as more scratches are introduced -, and so cannot be used to determine rigid body disparity between two images taken – for instance – months apart. 

A better method, therefore, may be to use the boundary markers found in the previous stages. This should be more reliable, as they are strong, already identified features, which are not as subject to change in appearance over time than the region actually within the working area.

Thus, in the test case, the rigid-body disparity is found by comparing the boundary marker masks of the current and archive image, rather than the images themselves.
